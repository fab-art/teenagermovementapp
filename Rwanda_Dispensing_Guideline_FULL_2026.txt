INTRODUCTION AND PURPOSE
This dispensing guideline has been developed to support pharmacists, prescribers, and pharmacy technicians in Rwanda in the safe and effective dispensing of reimbursable medicines under the RHIA (Rwanda Health Insurance Authority) scheme. For each therapeutic class and subclass, this guideline provides detailed pathophysiology of the conditions treated, mechanisms of action of relevant drug classes, and specific dispensing guidance critical for optimal patient outcomes.
Medicines are organized according to the WHO Anatomical Therapeutic Chemical (ATC) classification system. Each main ATC class section includes a description of the disease mechanisms, the pharmacological basis of treatment, and practical dispensing notes. Drug tables list generic names (International Non-proprietary Names, INN) alongside brand designations available in Rwanda, with strength and dosage form information.
IMPORTANT: This guideline is an educational and clinical decision-support tool. All prescribing and dispensing must remain within the scope of practice of each professional, and clinical judgment should always be applied to individual patient circumstances. Current national treatment guidelines and the RHIA reimbursement policies should be consulted for definitive guidance.
A. ALIMENTARY TRACT AND METABOLISM
A01 .STOMATOLOGICAL PREPARATIONS
STOMATOLOGICAL PREPARATIONS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Dental caries, gingivitis, and oral candidiasis are primary oral pathologies. Caries develops when acidogenic bacteria (Streptococcus mutans) metabolize dietary carbohydrates to lactic acid, demineralizing tooth enamel. Gingivitis involves bacterial biofilm formation triggering inflammatory cascade (IL-1, TNF-α). Oral candidiasis occurs when Candida albicans overgrowth disrupts normal oral flora, typically in immunocompromised states or antibiotic use.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Fluoride strengthens enamel via fluorapatite formation, increasing acid resistance. Antimicrobials (chlorhexidine, hexetidine) disrupt bacterial cell membranes. Antifungals (clotrimazole, miconazole) inhibit fungal ergosterol synthesis. Anti-inflammatory agents reduce gingival edema and bleeding.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Fluoride products: counsel on spitting out after use (toxicity risk in children <6 yrs). Antifungals: full course needed (7-14 days) even if symptoms resolve. Chlorhexidine: causes staining; avoid in nursing mothers (absorption into breast milk). Patient education on plaque removal essential for compliance.

A02  DRUGS FOR ACID RELATED DISORDERS
DRUGS FOR ACID-RELATED DISORDERS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
GERD, peptic ulcers, and Zollinger-Ellison syndrome result from excessive gastric acid. Normal gastric pH 1.5-2 protects against pathogens but damages mucosa if protective barriers fail. H. pylori infection disrupts mucin layer. Acid causes protein denaturation, epithelial necrosis, and bleeding. NSAIDs inhibit cytoprotective prostaglandins, increasing ulcer risk.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Antacids (Al/Mg hydroxides): neutralize acid via buffering, onset 5 min, duration 30-60 min. H2-blockers (cimetidine, famotidine): block histamine-H2 receptors on parietal cells, reducing acid 60-70%. PPIs (omeprazole, pantoprazole): irreversibly inhibit H+/K+-ATPase, reducing acid 90%+, onset 3-5 days. Mucoprotective agents (sucralfate): polymerize in acidic pH to coat ulcer base.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Antacids: take 1-3 hours after meals; separate from other drugs by 2 hrs (absorption interference). H2-blockers: optimal at bedtime or before largest meal. PPIs: take 30-60 min before food, swallow whole (enteric coating); counsel 4-8 week healing period. Avoid NSAIDs during treatment. Monitor long-term PPI use for B12/magnesium depletion.

A03A DRUGS FOR FUNCTIONAL BOWEL  DISORDERS
ANTISPASMODICS AND ANTICHOLINERGICS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
IBS, colitis, and GI spasm involve smooth muscle hypercontractility. Acetylcholine released from parasympathetic neurons binds muscarinic receptors on smooth muscle, triggering Ca2+ influx and contraction. Dysregulation causes cramping, diarrhea, and abdominal pain.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Anticholinergics (scopolamine, dicyclomine): block muscarinic-M3 receptors on smooth muscle, preventing acetylcholine-induced contraction. Onset 30-60 min. Antispasmodics (mebeverine, alverine): direct smooth muscle relaxation via calcium channel blockade.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Anticholinergics: counsel on dry mouth, urinary retention, blurred vision; avoid in glaucoma/BPH. Take 30-60 min before meals. Contraindicated in myasthenia gravis, tachycardia. Mebeverine: effective only in IBS (not organic disease); full trial 2-3 weeks needed.

A04A ANTIEMETICS AND ANTINAUSEANTS
ANTIEMETICS AND ANTINAUSEANTS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Nausea/vomiting triggered via chemoreceptor trigger zone (dopamine, serotonin, histamine, NK1 receptors), vestibular system (histamine), or vagal afferents (chemotherapy, gastroenteritis). Vomiting center in medulla integrates signals. Uncontrolled emesis causes electrolyte loss, dehydration, aspiration risk.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Antihistamines (promethazine, cinnarizine): block H1 receptors in vomiting center; anticholinergic properties; cross BBB. 5-HT3 antagonists (ondansetron, granisetron): block serotonin receptors in chemoreceptor trigger zone; highly effective for chemotherapy/post-op nausea. Metoclopramide: D2 antagonist + 5-HT4 agonist; prokinetic effect via acetylcholine sensitization. NK1 antagonists: block substance P.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Metoclopramide: 30 min before meals; risk of tardive dyskinesia with prolonged use (>3 months). Ondansetron: given before chemotherapy/surgery; IV/oral forms. Anticholinergics: avoid in elderly (confusion, retention). Counsel on drowsiness/dizziness with antihistamines.

A05 BILE AND LIVER THERAPY
BILE ACIDS AND LIPID-MODIFYING AGENTS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Cholestasis (intrahepatic/extrahepatic) leads to bile acid accumulation, causing pruritus, malabsorption, and hepatocyte injury. Hyperlipidemia (familial or acquired) increases atherosclerosis risk via LDL oxidation and foam cell formation. Gallstones form from cholesterol supersaturation.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Bile acids (ursodeoxycholic acid): reduce cholesterol saturation in bile, promote cholesterol dissolution in gallstones; also reduce intestinal cholesterol absorption. Statins: inhibit HMG-CoA reductase (rate-limiting step in cholesterol synthesis); reduce LDL 30-50%, raise HDL. Fibrates: activate PPAR-α, increase lipoprotein lipase, lower triglycerides 40-50%. Ezetimibe: blocks NPC1L1 cholesterol transporter in intestine.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Ursodeoxycholic acid: 2-3 years for stone dissolution; monitor with ultrasound. Statins: take at bedtime (max HMG-CoA activity at night); monitor LFTs + creatine kinase (myopathy risk). Fibrates + statins = myopathy risk (monitor). Ezetimibe: can combine with statins; separate dosing by 2 hours from bile acid sequestrants.

A06 DRUGS FOR CONSTIPATION
LAXATIVES
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Constipation results from slow colonic transit, inadequate fluid intake, or weak defecation reflexes. Severe constipation causes fecal impaction, bowel obstruction, or megacolon. Long-term straining increases hemorrhoid/diverticular disease risk.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Bulk laxatives (psyllium, methylcellulose): absorb water, increase stool volume and trigger reflex. Onset 12-72 hrs. Osmotic laxatives (lactulose, polyethylene glycol): retain water in lumen via osmosis; lactulose metabolized to SCFA, acidifying colon (inhibits ammonia reabsorption—useful in hepatic encephalopathy). Stimulant laxatives (bisacodyl, senna): activate myenteric plexus via altered electrolyte transport; onset 6-12 hrs. Stool softeners (docusate): reduce surface tension, improve water penetration.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Bulk laxatives: require 8-10 cups water daily; ineffective if dehydrated. Lactulose: titrate to 2-3 soft stools/day; can cause bloating/flatulence (fermentation). Bisacodyl: avoid >1 week (mucosal damage risk). PEG: mix powder with 4-8 oz liquid; used pre-operatively. Docusate alone ineffective; combine with other agents.

A07. ANTIDIARRHEALS , INTESTINAL ANTIINFLAMMATORY, AND ANTIINFECTIVE AGENTS
ANTIDIARRHEALS, INTESTINAL ANTI-INFLAMMATORY/ANTI-INFECTIVE AGENTS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Acute diarrhea (infectious/toxin-mediated): bacterial toxins (E. coli LT, cholera toxin) activate adenylyl cyclase → ↑ cAMP → Cl- secretion exceeds absorption. Inflammatory diarrhea (IBD, Crohn's): Th1/Th17 cells activate lamina propria macrophages → TNF-α, IL-6, IL-8 → epithelial damage. Chronic diarrhea (IBS, malabsorption): altered motility or reduced absorptive capacity.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Antimotility agents (loperamide, diphenoxylate): μ-opioid agonists on myenteric plexus → ↓ peristalsis, ↑ absorption time. Antimicrobials (ciprofloxacin, azithromycin): target pathogenic bacteria (invasive E. coli, Campylobacter, Salmonella). Aminosalicylates (mesalamine, sulfasalazine): topical anti-inflammatory; mesalamine releases 5-ASA in colon. Corticosteroids: systemic immunosuppression. Antibiotics (metronidazole): for C. difficile, entamoeba.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
AVOID loperamide in: bloody diarrhea (toxic megacolon risk), invasive pathogens, IBD exacerbations. Ciprofloxacin 3 days for traveler's diarrhea. Mesalamine: 3-4 g/day maintenance for remission. Sulfasalazine: monitor CBC (hemolysis risk in G6PD). Corticosteroids: taper gradually to prevent adrenal crisis.

A09 DIGESTIVES INCLUDING ENZYMES
DIGESTIVE ENZYMES
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Pancreatic insufficiency (CF, chronic pancreatitis, post-gastrectomy): inadequate lipase, amylase, protease → steatorrhea, malabsorption, weight loss. Enzyme deficiency causes nutrient loss, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Pancreatic enzyme supplements (pancreatin, pancrelipase): contain lipase, amylase, protease extracted from porcine pancreas. Replace deficient endogenous enzymes; enteric-coated formulations survive stomach acid.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Dose titrated to clinical response (stools/steatorrhea). Take with meals. Enteric-coated forms MUST NOT be crushed (acid inactivation). Monitor for efficacy (weight gain, reduction in steatorrhea). Fish-oil supplements may help absorption.

A10 DRUGS USED IN DIABETES
INSULIN AND BLOOD GLUCOSE LOWERING DRUGS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Type 2 DM: pancreatic β-cell dysfunction + insulin resistance. Hyperglycemia causes non-enzymatic glycation of proteins → microvascular (retinopathy, nephropathy, neuropathy) and macrovascular (MI, stroke) complications. Osmotic hyperglycemia causes polyuria/polydipsia. Ketoacidosis in Type 1 DM when insulin absent.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Insulins: exogenous insulin replaces deficient endogenous; rapid (lispro, aspart), NPH (intermediate), basal (glargine, detemir). Mechanism: glucose uptake via GLUT4 translocators; glycogen/protein synthesis activation. Metformin: ↓ hepatic gluconeogenesis via AMP activation; ↑ insulin sensitivity. Sulfonylureas (glyburide, glipizide): K-ATP channel closure on β-cells → insulin release. Dipeptidyl peptidase-4 inhibitors (sitagliptin): ↓ GLP-1 breakdown → ↑ incretin effect. SGLT2 inhibitors (empagliflozin): block renal glucose reabsorption → increased urinary glucose excretion. GLP-1 agonists: as above (A08).
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Insulin: teach injection technique, site rotation (prevent lipohypertrophy), storage (refrigerate; room temp max 28 days), hypoglycemia recognition (tremor, sweating, confusion—treat with 15g fast carbs). Metformin: take with meals (GI upset); monitor renal function (eGFR <30 = CI); risk of lactic acidosis (rare). Sulfonylureas: hypoglycemia risk; counsel on missed meals. SGLT2i: monitor for DKA (rare), yeast infections, UTIs. Dose insulin based on pre/post-meal glucose log.

B. BLOOD AND BLOOD FORMING ORGANS
B01 ANTITHROMBOTICS AGENTS
ANTITHROMBOTIC AGENTS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Thrombosis forms via Virchow's triad: vessel damage (atherosclerosis, surgery), stasis (AF, immobility), and hypercoagulability (cancer, thrombophilia). Platelet aggregation → fibrin mesh formation → vessel occlusion. Arterial thrombosis (MI, stroke) vs. venous thrombosis (DVT, PE).
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Antiplatelet drugs: Aspirin → irreversible COX inhibition → ↓ TXA2 (platelet aggregation). P2Y12 inhibitors (clopidogrel, ticagrelor) → block ADP-induced platelet activation. GPIIb/IIIa inhibitors (abciximab) → block fibrinogen bridge. Anticoagulants: Unfractionated heparin (UFH) → potentiates antithrombin III → Factors IIa, Xa inhibition (rapid; IV/SC). Low-molecular-weight heparins (LMWH, enoxaparin) → selective Xa inhibition; SC, longer half-life. Warfarin → vitamin K antagonist → ↓ Factors II, VII, IX, X synthesis (slow; requires INR monitoring). DOACs (apixaban, rivaroxaban, dabigatran) → direct Xa/IIa inhibition; oral; fixed dosing; no monitoring.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Aspirin: GI upset (take with food); bleeding risk (hold 5-7 days pre-op). Clopidogrel: irreversible effect (≥5 days before surgery). UFH: monitor aPTT (therapeutic 1.5-2.5x baseline); risk of HIT (thrombocytopenia + thrombosis). LMWH: fixed dosing based on weight; monitor anti-Xa if renal impairment (CrCl <30). Warfarin: counsels INR checks (target 2-3 usually), dietary vitamin K consistency, many drug interactions. DOACs: contraindicated in mechanical prosthetic valves; monitor renal function.

C01 CARDIAC THERAPY
CARDIAC GLYCOSIDES AND INOTROPES
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Heart failure: decreased cardiac contractility (↓ CO) triggers compensatory mechanisms (SNS, RAAS, fluid retention) that worsen congestion. Systolic HF: weak contraction. Diastolic HF: stiff ventricle → impaired filling.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Cardiac glycosides (digoxin): inhibit Na+/K+-ATPase → ↑ intracellular Na+ → reduced Na+/Ca2+ exchanger activity → ↑ intracellular Ca2+ → enhanced contraction. Also vagomimetic (↓ conduction). Inotropes (dobutamine, milrinone): β1 agonism (dobutamine) or PDE3 inhibition (milrinone) → ↑ cAMP → ↑ contractility + vasodilation.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Digoxin: narrow therapeutic window (0.5-2 ng/mL); toxicity → arrhythmias, GI upset, visual disturbances. Monitor renal function (accumulates if CrCl <30). Many drug interactions (amiodarone, verapamil increase levels). Baseline EKG needed. Inotropes: IV only; hospital use; monitor BP, HR, for ischemia.

C02 ANTIHYPERTENSIVE DRUGS
ANTIHYPERTENSIVE AGENTS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Hypertension (>140/90 mmHg) causes vessel remodeling via increased wall stress → smooth muscle hypertrophy → reduced lumen. Endothelial dysfunction → reduced NO → vasoconstriction. Chronic HTN causes LVH, stroke, MI, renal disease.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
ACE inhibitors (enalapril, lisinopril): block ACE → ↓ angiotensin II (potent vasoconstrictor) + ↑ bradykinin. ARBs (losartan, valsartan): AT1 receptor antagonism → similar effect without bradykinin buildup. Beta-blockers (metoprolol, atenolol): β1 antagonism → ↓ HR, contractility, renin release. Calcium channel blockers (amlodipine, diltiazem): ↓ vascular smooth muscle Ca influx → vasodilation. Thiazide diuretics (HCTZ, chlorthalidone): ↓ blood volume + vasodilation; hypokalemia/hyperglycemia risk. Alpha-blockers (doxazosin): α1 antagonism → vasodilation.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
ACEi: counsel on dry cough (bradykinin; 10-20% incidence). Monitor K+ (hyperkalemia risk). ARBs: no cough; safer in pregnancy than ACEi (teratogenic). Beta-blockers: taper gradually (rebound HTN/MI if stopped abruptly); avoid in asthma/COPD. CCBs: grapefruit interaction (↑ levels); ankle edema common. Thiazides: monitor glucose, lipids, K+, uric acid. Start low dose; combine agents for synergy.

C03 DIURETIC DRUGS
DIURETICS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Edema (pulmonary, peripheral) or fluid overload in HF, renal disease, cirrhosis results from sodium retention + water follows osmotically.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Loop diuretics (furosemide): inhibit Na-K-2Cl cotransporter in loop of Henle → ↑ Na excretion (potent; onset 1 hr); high K+ wasting. Thiazides (HCTZ): distal tubule Na-Cl inhibition (moderate potency; 6-12 hr onset). K-sparing diuretics (spironolactone, amiloride): aldosterone antagonism or K channel blockade → minimal K wasting. Osmotic diuretics (mannitol): osmotic effect → water excretion (used for cerebral edema, acute kidney injury prevention).
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Loop diuretics: check daily weights (goal 1-2 lbs/day loss); K+ monitoring essential. Doses escalate with fluid retention. Thiazides: monitor for electrolyte derangements (hyponatremia, hypokalemia). K-sparing: contraindicated in renal impairment (hyperkalemia risk); monitor K+ closely. Osmotic: IV administration; risk of hyperkalemia, dehydration if overused.

PERIPHERAL VASODILATORS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Peripheral arterial disease: atherosclerotic occlusion → reduced blood flow → tissue ischemia, claudication (leg pain with exercise).
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Phosphodiesterase inhibitors (pentoxifylline): ↓ PDE → ↑ cAMP → improved RBC deformability + reduced platelet aggregation + vasodilation. D.DERMATOLOGICALS
D01 ANTIFUNGAL FOR DERMATOLOGICAL USE
ANTIFUNGALS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Fungal infections (tinea, candidiasis) result from dermatophyte or yeast colonization. Warm, moist environments (groins, feet) favor growth. Immunosuppression (HIV, diabetes) enables systemic infection.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Azoles (miconazole, clotrimazole, ketoconazole): inhibit lanosterol 14α-demethylase → ↓ ergosterol synthesis → fungal cell death. Topical = fungistatic; systemic = fungicidal. Polyenes (amphotericin B, nystatin): bind ergosterol → pore formation → cell lysis (fungicidal). Allylamines (terbinafine): inhibit squalene epoxidase → ↓ ergosterol precursors.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Topical azoles: apply BID-TID x 2-4 weeks (depend on extent); continue 1-2 weeks after lesions disappear (prevent relapse). Nystatin: apply QID; risk of contact dermatitis. Terbinafine: PO for nail infections (12 weeks); monitor LFTs. Systemic azoles: monitor LFTs (hepatotoxicity risk).

D03 TREATMENT OF WOUNDS AND ULCERS
PREPARATIONS FOR WOUNDS AND ULCERS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Chronic wounds (ulcers, burns) fail to heal due to impaired angiogenesis, inadequate matrix remodeling, or persistent infection.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Growth factors (PDGF, FGF): promote fibroblast proliferation + angiogenesis. Proteolytic enzymes (collagenase, papain): debride necrotic tissue. Antimicrobials (iodine, silver): prevent infection.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Enzymatic debridement: remove eschar 1st (impedes absorption). Apply to wound only (avoid surrounding skin—chemical burn). Regular dressing changes. Gauge effectiveness via healing rate; switch if no improvement in 4 weeks.

Psoriasis: Th17-mediated autoimmunity → keratinocyte hyperproliferation (normal 28 days → 3-4 days) → scaling plaques. Eczema: impaired skin barrier + Th2 inflammation → itching, erythema.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Corticosteroids: ↓ T-cell infiltration + cytokine production. Calcineurin inhibitors (tacrolimus, pimecrolimus): block NFAT → T-cell inactivation (steroid-sparing). Retinoids (tretinoin, acitretin): normalize keratinocyte differentiation. Vitamin D analogs (calcipotriene): ↓ keratinocyte proliferation + ↑ differentiation. Biologics (TNF inhibitors, IL-17 antagonists): systemic immunosuppression.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Topical steroids: taper gradually (rebound flare); don't use on face long-term (atrophy). Calcineurin inhibitors: safer for face; no atrophy risk. Retinoids: teratogenic (pregnancy CI); photosensitivity (sunscreen required). Methotrexate (oral): slow response (6-12 weeks); monitor CBC, LFTs. Biologics: expensive; reserved for severe disease.

H03 THYROID THERAPY
THYROID THERAPY
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Hypothyroidism (autoimmune/iodine deficiency): ↓ thyroid hormone → ↓ metabolic rate, cold intolerance, weight gain, bradycardia, cognitive slowing.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Levothyroxine (T4): peripheral conversion to T3 (active form); long half-life (7 days) allows once-daily dosing. T3 (liothyronine): direct active form; faster onset, shorter duration.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Levothyroxine: take on empty stomach, 30-60 min before food (absorption reduced by food, calcium, iron). Dose titrated to TSH (goal 0.5-2.5 mIU/L typically). Takes 6-8 weeks to reach steady-state. Many drug interactions (iron, calcium, PPIs ↓ absorption). Monitor symptoms + labs.

J01 ANTIBACTERIAL DRUGS
SYSTEMICALLY USED ANTIBACTERIAL AGENTS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Bacterial infections: pathogenic bacteria cause inflammation via toxins, LPS, or direct invasion. Severity ranges from minor (UTI, otitis) to life-threatening (sepsis, meningitis, endocarditis).
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Beta-lactams (amoxicillin, cephalexin, ceftriaxone): inhibit transpeptidase → cell wall synthesis inhibition (bactericidal). Macrolides (erythromycin, azithromycin): 30S ribosome inhibition → protein synthesis (bacteriostatic). Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, levofloxacin): DNA gyrase/topoisomerase IV inhibition → DNA replication failure (bactericidal). Tetracyclines (doxycycline, tetracycline): 30S inhibition (bacteriostatic). Aminoglycosides (gentamicin): 30S inhibition → misreading (bactericidal at high concentrations).
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Beta-lactams: full course 7-14 days (resistance development + relapse if shortened). Monitor for allergic reactions (cross-reactivity with cephalosporins ~1%). Macrolides: GI upset (take with food); QT prolongation risk (monitor). Fluoroquinolones: tendon rupture risk (elderly, corticosteroid users); avoid in children (cartilage concern). Tetracyclines: photosensitivity (sunscreen); esophageal ulcers if swallowed dry. Aminoglycosides: dose by weight/renal function; nephrotoxicity + ototoxicity monitoring.

■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Systemic fungal infections (candemia, aspergillosis, cryptococcosis): opportunistic in neutropenia, HIV, post-transplant states.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Azoles (fluconazole, voriconazole): as D01 (systemic penetration better in azoles than topical). Polyenes (amphotericin B): lipid formulations (liposomal ampho B) reduce nephrotoxicity vs. conventional. Echinocandins (caspofungin): inhibit β-1,3-glucan synthesis → unique fungal cell wall target.

J04 ANTIMYCOBACTERIALS
ANTIVIRALS FOR SYSTEMIC USE
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Viral infections: intracellular obligate parasites; range from acute (influenza, COVID) to chronic (HIV, HBV, HCV).
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Nucleoside analogs (acyclovir, AZT): incorporate into viral DNA → chain termination. Protease inhibitors (ritonavir, lopinavir): HIV protease inhibition → immature virion formation. NNRTI (efavirenz, rilpivirine): reverse transcriptase allosteric inhibition. Integrase inhibitors (dolutegravir): HIV integration into host genome blockade. Direct-acting antivirals for HCV (sofosbuvir, daclatasvir): NS5B/NS5A protein targeting.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Antiretrovirals: absolute adherence required (>95%) for resistance prevention. Monitor CD4, viral load. Drug interactions (ritonavir is potent CYP3A4 inhibitor—many interactions). Side effects vary (GI with protease inhibitors, rash with NNRTIs). Acyclovir: IV for severe infections; high-dose prophylaxis in immunocompromised.

■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Gout: monosodium urate crystal deposition in joints → NLRP3 inflammasome activation → IL-1β release → intense inflammation.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES

Normal SNpc dopaminergic neurons project via nigrostriatal pathway to striatum (putamen/caudate) → dopamine release onto D1 receptors (direct pathway → excitation → movement facilitation) and D2 receptors (indirect pathway → inhibition → movement suppression). Net effect of DA: disinhibits thalamus → cortical motor activation. With >60-70% SNpc neuron loss + >80% striatal dopamine depletion → clinical PD: resting tremor (4-6 Hz, 'pill-rolling'), rigidity (lead-pipe/cogwheel), bradykinesia (slowness/poverty of movement), postural instability. Non-motor features: autonomic dysfunction (constipation, orthostatic hypotension, urinary urgency), sleep disturbance (REM sleep behaviour disorder — often precedes motor symptoms by years), hyposmia, depression, cognitive impairment → dementia. Alpha-synuclein spreads in Braak stages (gut → brain via vagus; olfactory bulb → limbic → neocortex). Secondary parkinsonism: drug-induced (dopamine antagonists — antipsychotics, metoclopramide), vascular, post-encephalitic, MSA, PSP, CBD.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Levodopa (L-DOPA): amino acid precursor to dopamine; crosses blood-brain barrier via large neutral amino acid transporter (LAT1) — dopamine itself does NOT cross BBB. In brain: aromatic amino acid decarboxylase (AADC) converts L-DOPA → dopamine in remaining nigrostriatal terminals and astrocytes. Peripherally: 95% converted to dopamine by AADC (nausea, vomiting, hypotension, arrhythmias). Carbidopa/benserazide: peripheral AADC inhibitors (do not cross BBB) → block peripheral L-DOPA conversion → ↑ brain L-DOPA availability + ↓ peripheral dopamine side effects → dose of L-DOPA reduced 75%. Levodopa/carbidopa (Sinemet, Madopar): gold standard pharmacotherapy. Long-term L-DOPA: dyskinesias (involuntary choreiform movements) from pulsatile stimulation of sensitized striatal DA receptors; motor fluctuations ('wearing off' — end-of-dose deterioration; 'on-off' phenomenon). Dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole, rotigotine patch, cabergoline, bromocriptine): directly stimulate D2/D3 receptors (pramipexole/ropinirole D3>D2; cabergoline D2). Less pulsatile stimulation vs L-DOPA → lower dyskinesia risk. Monotherapy in younger PD patients to delay L-DOPA. Less effective than L-DOPA. Non-ergot (pramipexole, ropinirole) preferred over ergot (cabergoline, bromocriptine) — no fibrosis risk. MAO-B inhibitors (selegiline, rasagiline, safinamide): selectively inhibit MAO-B (predominant form in striatum) → ↓ dopamine breakdown → ↑ striatal dopamine. Modest symptomatic + possible neuroprotective effect. COMT inhibitors (entacapone, tolcapone): inhibit catechol-O-methyltransferase → ↓ peripheral L-DOPA degradation → prolonged L-DOPA half-life → smoother motor effect → ↓ 'wearing off'. Entacapone co-formulated with L-DOPA/carbidopa (Stalevo). Tolcapone: more potent but rare fatal hepatotoxicity → restricted use. Amantadine: weak NMDA antagonist → reduces dyskinesias; also weak dopaminergic. Anticholinergics (trihexyphenidyl, benztropine): reduce relative cholinergic excess (dopamine normally inhibits striatal cholinergic interneurons — loss of DA → cholinergic hyperactivity → tremor/rigidity). Most useful for tremor in young patients. Avoid in elderly (anticholinergic side effects, cognitive impairment). Apomorphine (SC): potent non-selective DA agonist; subcutaneous injection or pump; used for rescue from 'off' episodes or continuous infusion via pump in advanced PD.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
L-DOPA/carbidopa: initiate low (100/25 mg TID) and titrate every 1-2 weeks. Take 30-60 min before meals or 2 hours after (protein competes with L-DOPA for LAT1 transport → delayed or reduced absorption with protein-rich meals; protein redistribution diet in some patients). Nausea: common initially (domperidone — NOT metoclopramide/prochlorperazine/haloperidol as these cross BBB and worsen parkinsonism); takes 2-4 weeks to resolve. Motor fluctuations: options include increasing dose frequency, adding COMT inhibitor (entacapone) or MAO-B inhibitor, continuous duodenal infusion, or deep brain stimulation (DBS). Controlled-release L-DOPA: less predictable absorption — generally not preferred except overnight. NEVER abruptly stop L-DOPA (neuroleptic malignant syndrome-like syndrome — hyperthermia, rigidity, confusion). Dopamine agonists: IMPULSE CONTROL DISORDERS (ICDs) — pathological gambling, hypersexuality, binge eating, compulsive shopping in 10-17%; screen at every visit; dose reduction or switch if develops (resolve on stopping usually). Orthostatic hypotension (all DA agents, L-DOPA). Daytime somnolence, sleep attacks (sudden sleep onset — driving risk — must counsel patient and consider driving fitness; risk highest with dopamine agonists). Leg edema (pramipexole). Hallucinations and psychosis — dose reduction; if persisting, clozapine (12.5-25 mg at night — unique non-dopamine-blocking antipsychotic, safe in PD) or quetiapine (off-label). Selegiline: mild amphetamine metabolites (insomnia — avoid evening doses). Rasagiline: avoid tyramine-rich foods in high doses (weak MAOI properties — hypertensive crisis). Interactions: tramadol, SSRIs, SNRIs + MAO-B inhibitors → serotonin syndrome. Apomorphine: severe nausea/vomiting without domperidone pretreatment; rotate injection sites (subcutaneous nodules); QTc prolongation. DBS: refer advanced PD with good L-DOPA response but disabling motor fluctuations/dyskinesias. Neuroprotection: no confirmed neuroprotective treatment in clinical use.

N05A ANTIPSYCHOTIC  DRUGS
ANTIPSYCHOTIC DRUGS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Psychotic disorders — schizophrenia, schizoaffective disorder, psychotic depression, bipolar mania — share positive symptoms (hallucinations, delusions, thought disorder) from excessive dopaminergic transmission in mesolimbic pathway (VTA → nucleus accumbens); and negative symptoms (anhedonia, avolition, flat affect, social withdrawal) and cognitive dysfunction from dopaminergic hypofunction in mesocortical pathway (VTA → prefrontal cortex). Glutamate hypofunction (NMDA receptor dysfunction) also central — explains negative symptoms and cognitive features. Dopamine hypothesis: 1950s observation that chlorpromazine (D2 blocker) relieved psychosis; all effective antipsychotics block D2 receptors (minimum 60-65% D2 occupancy for antipsychotic effect; >80% → EPS). Serotonergic pathway (5-HT2A in prefrontal cortex) modulates dopamine release — 5-HT2A antagonism (atypicals) restores mesocortical dopamine → ↓ negative symptoms.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
First-generation (typical) antipsychotics (FGAs): Haloperidol, chlorpromazine, fluphenazine, perphenazine, trifluoperazine, thioridazine. Potent D2 blockade (nigrostriatal → EPS; tuberoinfundibular → hyperprolactinaemia). Chlorpromazine: low-potency, more sedating, more anticholinergic. Haloperidol: high-potency, less sedating, more EPS. Second-generation (atypical) antipsychotics (SGAs): Risperidone, olanzapine, quetiapine, aripiprazole, clozapine, paliperidone, ziprasidone, amisulpride, lurasidone. D2 + 5-HT2A blockade (5-HT2A block in mesocortical pathway → DA release → ↓ negative symptoms; 5-HT2A block in nigrostriatal pathway → DA release → offsetting D2 block → ↓ EPS). Clozapine: uniquely effective for treatment-resistant schizophrenia (TRS); low D2 affinity/fast dissociation + broad receptor profile (H1, α1, M1, 5-HT2A); associated with agranulocytosis (requires mandatory haematological monitoring); weight gain + metabolic syndrome; seizures; myocarditis/cardiomyopathy; hypersalivation. Aripiprazole: D2/D3 partial agonist (functional antagonist at hyperdopaminergic sites, agonist at hypodopaminergic sites) — unique 'dopamine system stabilizer'; less weight gain, less prolactin elevation. Quetiapine: metabolized to norquetiapine (antidepressant properties — NE reuptake inhibition) → used for bipolar depression. Long-acting injectable (LAI/depot) formulations: paliperidone palmitate (monthly/3-monthly), risperidone microspheres (biweekly), haloperidol decanoate (monthly), aripiprazole lauroxil (monthly/6-weekly) → bypass first-pass, consistent plasma levels, better adherence in patients with insight/compliance issues.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Acute psychosis: oral or IM antipsychotic + benzodiazepine (lorazepam IM) for agitation. Haloperidol 5-10 mg IM for acute control. Extrapyramidal side effects (EPS): Acute dystonia (within hours-days): sustained painful muscle spasm, oculogyric crisis, torticollis — treat with IM/IV anticholinergic (benztropine 1-2 mg or diphenhydramine 25-50 mg) immediately. Akathisia (within days-weeks): intense subjective restlessness, inability to sit still — β-blocker (propranolol 10-30 mg TID) or mirtazapine; reduce dose; switch to SGA. Parkinsonism (weeks): bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor from D2 blockade — reduce dose; add procyclidine/trihexyphenidyl; switch to atypical. Tardive dyskinesia (TD — months-years): repetitive involuntary orofacial/limb movements from D2 receptor upregulation (supersensitivity) — REDUCE/STOP offending drug; may be irreversible; valbenazine/deutetrabenazine (VMAT2 inhibitors) approved for treatment. Neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS): life-threatening idiosyncratic reaction — hyperthermia, lead-pipe rigidity, autonomic instability (BP, HR), altered consciousness, ↑ CK. STOP antipsychotic immediately; supportive ICU care; dantrolene, bromocriptine. Metabolic syndrome (SGAs — especially olanzapine, clozapine): weight gain, dyslipidaemia, impaired fasting glucose/T2DM, hypertension. Monitor: weight/BMI, waist circumference, fasting glucose, lipids, BP at baseline, 3 months, annually. Olanzapine: most weight gain of all SGAs (5-10 kg/year). QTc prolongation: all antipsychotics to varying degrees — ECG at baseline; especially sertindole, ziprasidone, pimozide — avoid with other QTc-prolonging agents. Prolactin elevation (haloperidol, risperidone, amisulpride ↑↑): galactorrhoea, amenorrhoea, sexual dysfunction, gynecomastia, ↓ bone density. Clozapine monitoring: MANDATORY weekly FBC for first 18 weeks → biweekly for next 26 weeks → monthly (absolute neutrophil count: suspend if ANC <2000, stop if <1500 and NEVER rechallenge with clozapine if ANC <1000). Clozapine dispensing: ONLY through restricted dispensing program (CPMS/CMS). Titrate slowly from 12.5 mg → max 300-900 mg/day. Sedation, orthostatic hypotension, hypersalivation, constipation (severe — ileus risk; prescribe laxatives), urinary incontinence, weight gain, seizures (>600 mg/day risk — add valproate prophylaxis). Myocarditis screening (troponin + CRP weeks 1-4). LAI antipsychotics: ensure adequate plasma levels from oral drug before first LAI injection (especially paliperidone palmitate — established overlap not needed). Administer by trained staff; gluteal or deltoid; observe after for 3 hours (injection-related reactions).

N05B ANXIOLYTIC DRUGS
ANXIOLYTIC DRUGS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Anxiety disorders (GAD, panic disorder, social anxiety, specific phobias, PTSD) involve: ↑ amygdala reactivity (fear circuitry hyperactivity), ↓ prefrontal cortical inhibition of amygdala, altered HPA axis (CRH hypersecretion → cortisol), and GABAergic/serotonergic dysfunction. GAD: chronic uncontrollable worry + physical symptoms (muscle tension, fatigue, insomnia, irritability, concentration difficulty). Panic disorder: episodic overwhelming fear with autonomic surge (palpitations, dyspnea, chest pain, dizziness, derealization, fear of dying/losing control). GABA (γ-aminobutyric acid) is the primary inhibitory CNS neurotransmitter: GABA-A receptor is a pentameric ligand-gated Cl⁻ channel → Cl⁻ influx → hyperpolarization → ↓ neuronal firing. Reduced GABAergic tone in amygdala → ↑ anxiety.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Benzodiazepines (diazepam, lorazepam, alprazolam, clonazepam, oxazepam, temazepam, midazolam): positive allosteric modulators of GABA-A at benzodiazepine-binding site (α-subunit adjacent to γ-subunit) → ↑ frequency of Cl⁻ channel opening (NOT amplitude — barbiturates ↑ duration) → hyperpolarization → ↓ amygdala/limbic hyperactivity → anxiolytic, sedative, muscle relaxant, anticonvulsant, amnesic. All BZDs are full agonists with similar mechanisms — differences are pharmacokinetic. Short-acting (lorazepam, oxazepam, temazepam): no active metabolites (safer in elderly/liver disease; less accumulation). Long-acting (diazepam, chlordiazepoxide): active metabolites (self-tapering, useful for BZD withdrawal). Azapirones (buspirone): 5-HT1A partial agonist in raphe nuclei → ↓ serotonergic transmission → anxiolytic (no sedation, no addiction, no respiratory depression, no GABAergic effects). Onset 2-4 weeks. Effective for GAD maintenance. Hydroxyzine: H1 antihistamine + anticholinergic → sedating anxiolytic; no addiction potential; used for acute anxiety and pruritus. Pregabalin: α2δ VGCC subunit binding → ↓ glutamate release (anxiolytic mechanism). Effective for GAD, social anxiety, PTSD. SSRIs/SNRIs: first-line for chronic anxiety disorders (see N06A) — onset 4-6 weeks.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Benzodiazepines: use for SHORT DURATIONS only (2-4 weeks maximum) due to: tolerance (rapidly develops to sedative/anxiolytic effects — does not develop to anticonvulsant/amnesic), physical dependence (GABA-A receptor downregulation — withdrawal syndrome on cessation), cognitive impairment (memory, attention, reaction time — driving risk), falls in elderly (3x ↑ hip fracture risk), abuse potential. Prescribe minimum effective dose, lowest potency, for defined short course. Withdrawal syndrome: rebound anxiety, insomnia, tremor, sweating, tachycardia, seizures (can be life-threatening with long-acting, high-dose BZD dependence — similar to alcohol withdrawal). Taper gradually over weeks-months (equivalent diazepam dose conversion → diazepam taper 10% reduction per week maximum). Lorazepam: no active metabolites — preferred in liver disease, elderly. Absorbed IM (diazepam not reliably absorbed IM — use IV). Buspirone: explain 2-4 week onset (patients expect immediate effect like BZDs — adherence problem). Prior BZD users respond less well. Pregabalin: titrate from 25-75 mg to 150-450 mg/day for anxiety. Abuse/misuse potential (especially in substance use disorder patients). Monitor weight (↑↑), dizziness, somnolence. Antidepressants (SSRIs/SNRIs) should be first-line for all chronic anxiety disorders with BZDs reserved only for adjunctive short-term use during initiation of SSRI.

N05C HYPNOTICS AND SEDATIVES DRUGS
HYPNOTICS AND SEDATIVES
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Insomnia: impaired sleep initiation, maintenance, or non-restorative sleep causing daytime impairment. Pathophysiology: hyperarousal model — ↑ activity of ascending arousal systems (noradrenergic locus coeruleus, serotonergic raphe, histaminergic tuberomammillary nucleus, orexinergic lateral hypothalamus, cholinergic basal forebrain) fails to be inhibited by sleep-promoting VLPO (ventrolateral preoptic nucleus — GABAergic/galaninergic). Chronic insomnia: conditioned hyperarousal (Spielman 3-P model: predisposing × precipitating × perpetuating factors). Sleep architecture: NREM (N1-light, N2-spindles/K-complexes, N3-slow wave) and REM cycling every 90 min; deep sleep predominantly early night, REM increases toward morning.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Z-drugs (zopiclone, zolpidem, zaleplon): non-benzodiazepine GABA-A positive allosteric modulators (similar to BZDs but more selective for α1-subunit containing GABA-A receptors — sedation, amnesia; less anxiolysis, muscle relaxation than classic BZDs). Short-acting (zaleplon t½ 1 hr, zolpidem t½ 2 hr, zopiclone t½ 5 hr). Melatonin: endogenous hormone secreted by pineal gland (MT1/MT2 receptor agonism) in response to darkness (circadian phase marker) → ↓ alertness + synchronization of circadian rhythm. Modest hypnotic effect; most useful for jet lag and circadian rhythm disorders. Ramelteon: MT1/MT2 agonist (more potent than melatonin). Low-dose doxepin: H1 antagonism at 3-6 mg → sleep maintenance insomnia (distinct from antidepressant doses). Orexin receptor antagonists (suvorexant): blocks OX1R and OX2R → ↓ arousal signal from lateral hypothalamus → promotes sleep without GABAergic CNS depression.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
First-line for chronic insomnia: Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) — superior to pharmacotherapy for long-term outcomes; includes stimulus control, sleep restriction, relaxation, cognitive restructuring, sleep hygiene. Pharmacotherapy adjunctive or short-term only. Z-drugs: similar risks to benzodiazepines (tolerance, dependence, cognitive impairment, parasomnia — sleepwalking/complex sleep-related behaviours with zolpidem; avoid with alcohol). Prescribe for 2-4 weeks. Elderly: ↑ risk falls, cognitive impairment, morning sedation (half-life prolonged in elderly) — use lowest dose; zopiclone 3.75 mg (half-tablet) in elderly (vs. 7.5 mg adults). Zolpidem gender difference: women clear more slowly → FDA lowered dose recommendation for women (5 mg vs 10 mg). Melatonin: 2-5 mg 30-60 min before bedtime; most useful for circadian phase disorders (shift work, jet lag). Low dependence risk. Antihistamines (diphenhydramine, promethazine): rapid tolerance (days) → ineffective for chronic insomnia; next-day sedation; anticholinergic (elderly — avoid per Beers criteria). Address comorbidities contributing to insomnia (pain, depression, anxiety, OSA, restless legs — treat RLS with dopamine agonists, iron supplementation).

N06A ANTIDEPRESSANTS DRUGS
ANTIDEPRESSANTS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Major depressive disorder (MDD): monoamine hypothesis — deficiency of serotonin (5-HT), norepinephrine (NE), and dopamine (DA) in limbic system and prefrontal cortex. More contemporary: neuroplasticity hypothesis — ↓ BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) → hippocampal atrophy and synaptic loss in PFC. HPA axis dysregulation (↑ cortisol → glucocorticoid receptor downregulation in hippocampus → ↓ negative feedback → chronically elevated cortisol → hippocampal damage). Inflammatory hypothesis — ↑ IL-6, TNF-α, IL-1 activate IDO (indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase) → tryptophan → kynurenine (not serotonin) → ↓ 5-HT + neurotoxic kynurenines. Treatment with antidepressants: latency of 4-6 weeks before clinical benefit (adaptation of receptor sensitivity, ↑ BDNF, neurogenesis required). All effective antidepressants ultimately ↑ monoaminergic transmission via different mechanisms. Dysthymia: chronic lower-grade depression ≥2 years. Bipolar depression: depressive episodes in bipolar disorder (antidepressants may trigger mania — use with mood stabilizer).
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
SSRIs (fluoxetine, sertraline, paroxetine, citalopram, escitalopram, fluvoxamine): inhibit SERT (serotonin transporter) → ↑ synaptic 5-HT → initial autoreceptor desensitization (weeks) → sustained 5-HT effect. Escitalopram/citalopram: most serotonin-selective (fewest drug interactions). Fluoxetine: longest half-life (t½ 1-4 days; active metabolite norfluoxetine t½ 7-15 days) → less discontinuation syndrome; potential for prolonged drug interactions. Paroxetine: most anticholinergic SSRI; shortest half-life → worst discontinuation syndrome; most CYP2D6 inhibition. SNRIs (venlafaxine, desvenlafaxine, duloxetine, levomilnacipran): inhibit SERT + NET (norepinephrine transporter) → ↑ 5-HT + NE. Duloxetine: balanced dual reuptake inhibition; also effective for neuropathic pain, fibromyalgia, stress urinary incontinence. Venlafaxine: primarily serotonergic at low doses; NE effect at ≥150 mg. TCAs (amitriptyline, clomipramine, nortriptyline, imipramine, desipramine): block SERT, NET, H1, M1 (anticholinergic), α1 (hypotension), Na⁺ channels (cardiac — QRS widening), + multiple others → broad receptor profile → sedation + weight gain + cardiac toxicity. Amitriptyline: highest anticholinergic; most sedating. Nortriptyline: active metabolite of amitriptyline; better tolerated; least hypotensive. MAOIs (phenelzine, tranylcypromine, moclobemide): irreversible MAO-A/B inhibition (phenelzine, tranylcypromine) → ↑ 5-HT, NE, DA; dietary tyramine (normally metabolized by intestinal/hepatic MAO) bypasses MAO → systemic absorption → ↑ NE → hypertensive crisis (palpitations, headache, flushing, stroke risk). Moclobemide: reversible MAO-A inhibitor (RIMA) — far safer tyramine interaction profile (reversible displacement by high tyramine). Mirtazapine: α2 autoreceptor antagonist → ↑ 5-HT and NE release; 5-HT2 and 5-HT3 antagonism (↓ nausea, ↓ insomnia); H1 blockade (sedation, weight gain). Effective as augmentation or monotherapy. Bupropion: NE and DA reuptake inhibitor (NDRI); no serotonergic → no sexual dysfunction; stimulant-like; also for smoking cessation. Seizure risk dose-dependent (CI in eating disorders, seizure history). Trazodone: SARI (serotonin antagonist and reuptake inhibitor); mainly 5-HT2A antagonism → insomnia (low dose 50-100 mg). Agomelatine: MT1/MT2 agonist + 5-HT2C antagonist → normalises circadian rhythmicity + ↑ NE/DA in PFC. Vortioxetine: SERT inhibitor + 5-HT1A agonist + 5-HT3/7 antagonist → broad serotonin modulation + cognitive benefits.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
SSRIs: start low and titrate gradually (activating effects [anxiety, agitation, insomnia] in first 2 weeks — counsel on transience). Explain 4-6 week full effect onset — critical for adherence (most patients stop prematurely). Sexual dysfunction (↓ libido, anorgasmia, delayed ejaculation) common and persistent (40-60%; consider dose reduction, drug holiday [not for fluoxetine], switching to bupropion/agomelatine/mirtazapine, or adding buspirone). Switching antidepressants: washout period especially from MAOIs (2 weeks after irreversible MAOI before SSRI; 5 weeks after fluoxetine before MAOI) to avoid serotonin syndrome. Serotonin syndrome: excessive serotonergic stimulation (SSRIs + tramadol/fentanyl/linezolid/triptans/St John's Wort/methamphetamine) → triad of cognitive changes (agitation, confusion), autonomic instability (fever, tachycardia, diaphoresis), and neuromuscular abnormalities (clonus [most specific], hyperreflexia, tremor, myoclonus). Treatment: stop serotonergic drugs, cyproheptadine (5-HT2A antagonist), supportive care. Duration of treatment: first episode uncomplicated MDD — minimum 6-12 months after remission before tapering (relapse prevention). Two+ episodes or high-risk features: 2 years to indefinite. Recurrent/chronic: long-term. TCAs: anticholinergic side effects (dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, blurred vision, confusion); orthostatic hypotension; cardiac — baseline ECG (QRS widening; QTc prolongation — arrhythmia risk especially in OD). Cardiotoxic in overdose (risk even at moderate excess — prescribe limited quantities). Avoid in elderly if possible (Beers criteria). Useful: amitriptyline for neuropathic pain (10-75 mg), migraine prophylaxis, insomnia at low doses. MAOIs: tyramine-restricted diet (aged cheeses, cured meats, pickled/fermented foods, red wine, soy sauce, Marmite, broad beans, overripe bananas) during use and 2 weeks after. Multiple drug interactions. Phenelzine/tranylcypromine: last resort for treatment-resistant/atypical depression (hypersomnia, hyperphagia, mood reactivity, rejection sensitivity). Mirtazapine: most sedating at 15 mg (H1 blockade dominant); paradoxically less sedating at higher doses (NE effect ↑). Weight gain substantial — use to advantage in depressed anorexic/insomniac patients. Bupropion: available as sustained release (300-450 mg/day). Lowers seizure threshold — contraindicated: bulimia/anorexia nervosa (electrolyte disturbance + seizure risk), history of seizures, abrupt alcohol withdrawal. Agomelatine: LFT monitoring (hepatotoxic — ALAT >3x ULN in ~1%; baseline LFTs + at 6, 12 weeks). No antidepressant shows superiority in efficacy — selection based on side-effect profile, patient comorbidities, prior response, and drug interactions.

ANTICHOLINESTERASE DRUGS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Alzheimer's disease (AD): most common dementia (60-70%); hallmarks: amyloid-β plaques (proteolytic products of amyloid precursor protein APP cleavage by β/γ-secretase) + neurofibrillary tangles (hyperphosphorylated tau → loss of microtubule function → neuronal death). Cholinergic deficit: early and profound loss of basal forebrain cholinergic neurons (nucleus basalis of Meynert) → ↓ acetylcholine in cortex and hippocampus → impaired memory encoding and attention. Myasthenia gravis (MG): autoimmune disease — anti-AChR antibodies (80%) or anti-MuSK antibodies → ↓ functional AChR at NMJ → characteristic fatigable weakness (ptosis, diplopia, dysarthria, dysphagia, limb weakness, respiratory muscle failure).
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (AChEIs): Donepezil, rivastigmine, galantamine — inhibit AChE (enzyme that cleaves ACh to choline + acetate in synaptic cleft) → ↑ synaptic ACh → enhanced cholinergic transmission. Donepezil: reversible, selective; once-daily. Rivastigmine: pseudo-irreversible; also inhibits BuChE; available as transdermal patch (fewer GI effects). Galantamine: also allosteric modulator of nicotinic AChRs (enhancing sensitivity to ACh). Pyridostigmine: reversible AChEI; quaternary ammonium → minimal CNS penetration; used for MG (NMJ — ↑ ACh availability → ↑ AChR stimulation → ↑ motor strength). Neostigmine: reversible AChEI; as pyridostigmine; IV → reversal of non-depolarizing neuromuscular block. Physostigmine: tertiary amine → crosses BBB; atropine overdose/anticholinergic toxidrome reversal.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Alzheimer's disease: donepezil 5 mg OD at bedtime (↑ to 10 mg after 4-6 weeks; 23 mg for moderate-severe AD). Rivastigmine patch: 4.6 mg/24hr → 9.5 mg/24hr; change daily; rotate sites. Side effects: GI (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea — commonest; mitigated by slow titration, taking with food, patch formulation), bradycardia (vagomimetic — caution in SSS, AV block), insomnia, muscle cramps. Set realistic expectations — AChEIs slow cognitive decline, they do NOT halt or reverse disease (modest but significant clinical benefit). Memantine (NMDA antagonist — N07AX): add-on in moderate-severe AD; ↓ excitotoxicity. Combination donepezil + memantine widely used. Pyridostigmine (MG): 30-120 mg PO q4-6h (titrate to symptom control; excessive doses → cholinergic crisis [↑↑ secretions, bradycardia, fasciculations, weakness — treat with atropine]). Distinguish myasthenic crisis (↑ weakness needing more pyridostigmine) from cholinergic crisis (too much pyridostigmine → excessive weakness) — edrophonium (Tensilon) test (ultra-short AChEI — ↑ strength transiently if myasthenic; ↑ secretions/weakness if cholinergic). Immunosuppression (prednisolone ± azathioprine/mycophenolate) needed for MG disease modification (AChEIs only symptomatic). Thymectomy if thymoma or age <60 generalised MG.

DRUGS USED IN ALCOHOL DEPENDENCE
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Alcohol use disorder (AUD): alcohol (ethanol) potentiates GABA-A receptors and inhibits NMDA glutamate receptors → central depression (sedation, anxiolysis, euphoria). Chronic alcohol use → GABA-A downregulation + NMDA upregulation (neuroadaptation) → tolerance. Acute alcohol withdrawal: removal of GABAergic enhancement + NMDA upregulation → ↑ CNS excitability → tremor (6-8 hrs) → seizures (24-48 hrs) → delirium tremens (48-72 hrs: hallucinations, severe autonomic instability, hyperthermia — 5% mortality untreated). Craving and relapse: dopaminergic reward pathways, endogenous opioid activation (alcohol ↑ β-endorphin release in VTA → DA activation → rewarding), and glutamate kindling. Alcohol-related disorders: Wernicke's encephalopathy (thiamine deficiency), alcoholic liver disease, cardiomyopathy, peripheral neuropathy.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Disulfiram (Antabuse): inhibits aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH) → acetaldehyde accumulates if alcohol consumed → aversion reaction (intense flushing, nausea, vomiting, palpitations, dyspnea, hypotension — highly unpleasant). Deterrent, not anti-craving. Requires daily supervised administration. Naltrexone: µ-opioid receptor antagonist → ↓ alcohol-induced β-endorphin release → ↓ DA reward → ↓ craving + ↓ positive reinforcement. Oral (50 mg daily) or IM monthly injection (Vivitrol). Nalmefene: µ-antagonist + κ-partial agonist → ↓ craving; taken 'as-needed' before anticipated drinking. Acamprosate: NMDA antagonism + GABA-A modulation → ↓ glutamate hyperexcitability (withdrawal-driven craving); most effective in post-withdrawal abstinence maintenance. Chlordiazepoxide/diazepam: for acute alcohol withdrawal management (BZD → cross-tolerance with GABA-A upregulation after alcohol removal → ↓ seizure/DT risk).
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Acute withdrawal: CIWA-Ar (Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment) score-driven BZD dosing or fixed-schedule reducing BZD. Chlordiazepoxide: 20-40 mg QID days 1-2 → taper over 7-10 days. IV diazepam for severe withdrawal/DTs. Seizure: diazepam IV first-line. Delirium tremens: ICU, IV BZDs, haloperidol for hallucinations, IV fluids, thiamine 100-500 mg IV TID x 3 days then oral. Thiamine (parenteral Pabrinex): BEFORE any glucose (glucose → pyruvate → depletes thiamine → precipitates Wernicke's). Disulfiram: informed consent essential; screen for cardiac/hepatic disease; counsel on reaction (even small amounts of alcohol: mouthwash, cooking wine, topical products, sauces). Contraindications: psychosis, severe CVD, uncontrolled HTN. Daily compliance supervision (partner-witnessed, dispensing pharmacy). Naltrexone: opioid-free for minimum 7-10 days before starting (precipitates acute withdrawal if opioid-dependent — urine toxicology first). Hepatic function tests (hepatotoxic at high doses). Contraindicated: acute hepatitis, severe liver disease. Acamprosate: start after detox; 333-666 mg TID (1332-1998 mg/day). Renally eliminated — dose adjust for CrCl. Combine pharmacotherapy with psychosocial treatment (CBT, motivational interviewing, 12-step programs — essential for sustained recovery).

N07CA  ANTIVERGO DRUGS
ANTIVERTIGO DRUGS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Vertigo: illusion of rotatory/translational movement; results from asymmetric vestibular input to the brain. BPPV (benign paroxysmal positional vertigo): canalith (otoconia) debris enters semicircular canal → inappropriate endolymph movement with head position changes → brief intense rotational vertigo (seconds). Ménière's disease: endolymphatic hydrops → increased endolymph volume → episodic vertigo (20 min - several hrs) + fluctuating sensorineural hearing loss + tinnitus + aural fullness. Vestibular neuritis: viral (HSV-1 reactivation) inflammation of vestibular nerve → acute prolonged (days) vertigo + nausea ± hearing loss. Central vertigo: posterior fossa lesion (stroke, tumour, MS, Chiari malformation) — must exclude in acute vertigo.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Histamine H1 antagonists (cinnarizine, cyclizine, dimenhydrinate, meclizine): H1 blockade in vestibular nuclei + vomiting center → ↓ vertigo/nausea. Also anticholinergic component (M1 blockade). Betahistine: H1 agonist + potent H3 antagonist → vasodilation of inner ear microvasculature (↑ labyrinthine blood flow) + ↓ histaminergic vestibular firing → reduces endolymphatic pressure (Ménière's); also enhances vestibular compensation. Prochlorperazine: D2 + H1 antagonist → potent antiemetic/antivertigo; used for acute severe attacks. Diazepam: GABAergic → ↓ brainstem vestibular reactivity; short-term acute vertigo.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
BPPV: Epley canalith repositioning manoeuvre (Dix-Hallpike to confirm → Epley to treat) is definitive treatment — no pharmacotherapy indicated. Vestibular neuritis: acute phase (days 1-3) — prochlorperazine IM/buccal for severe nausea/vertigo. Short BZD course (diazepam 2-5 mg TID) for 3-5 days only (prolonged BZD use impairs vestibular compensation — wean by day 3-5). Vestibular physiotherapy exercises (Cawthorne-Cooksey) critical for recovery — stimulate compensation. Ménière's disease: betahistine 16-24 mg TID (long-term maintenance; modest evidence for attack reduction). Dietary measures (low salt 1.5-2 g/day, avoid caffeine/alcohol). Diuretics (thiazide ± amiloride) for refractory. Cinnarizine: sedating (avoid driving); useful for motion sickness prevention (1-2 hrs before travel). Combined cinnarizine/dimenhydrinate (ARLEVERT): evidence for Ménière's acute management. Distinguish peripheral from central vertigo: 'HINTS exam' for acute vestibular syndrome (Head Impulse test, Nystagmus pattern, Test of Skew) — highly sensitive for posterior circulation stroke.

NO2A OPIOIDS
OPIOID ANALGESICS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
See N02B above for pain pathways. Opioid system: three families of endogenous opioids — enkephalins (δ receptors), endorphins (µ receptors), dynorphins (κ receptors). µ-opioid receptor (MOR): Gi-protein coupled → ↓ adenylyl cyclase (↓ cAMP) → inhibit presynaptic Ca²⁺ channels (↓ NT release) + activate postsynaptic K⁺ channels (hyperpolarization) → decreased dorsal horn pain transmission. Also in PAG (periaqueductal grey) → activate descending inhibitory pathways. Respiratory depression: µ-receptors in pre-Bötzinger complex → ↓ respiratory drive. Constipation: µ-receptors in enteric NS → ↓ peristalsis + ↑ sphincter tone. Tolerance: GRK (G-receptor kinase) phosphorylation → β-arrestin recruitment → receptor internalization → ↓ receptor density + ↓ Gi coupling → escalating doses needed. Physical dependence: CREB-mediated ↑ adenylyl cyclase (rebound ↑ cAMP on opioid withdrawal → withdrawal syndrome). Hyperalgesia: paradoxically, chronic high-dose opioids can increase pain sensitivity (NMDA activation, dynorphin upregulation).
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Morphine: prototype µ-agonist; hydrophilic → slow BBB penetration; active metabolite M-6-G (potent analgesic) accumulates in renal failure. Available IR (4-hourly) and ER/MR (12-hourly — MST Continus, Zomorph). Codeine: prodrug → morphine via CYP2D6 (10% conversion); antitussive at low doses. Avoid in children post-tonsillectomy (fatal respiratory depression in ultra-rapid metabolizers). Tramadol: as N02B. Pethidine (meperidine): norpethidine metabolite → CNS excitation, seizures; avoid >48 hrs, renal failure, MAOIs. Buprenorphine: partial µ-agonist (ceiling effect for respiratory depression → safer than full agonists) + κ-antagonist (↓ dysphoria); sublingually or transdermal; also used for OUD (opioid use disorder) maintenance. Fentanyl: lipophilic → rapid CNS onset; 100x morphine; IV (surgery/ICU), transdermal patch (72-hr chronic pain), oral transmucosal (breakthrough cancer pain), nasal spray. Hydromorphone: semi-synthetic, 5-7x morphine potency; renally safer than morphine (fewer active metabolites). Oxycodone: semi-synthetic; IR and ER formulations; similar to morphine. Methadone: long and unpredictable half-life (8-150 hrs); QTc prolongation; NMDA antagonism; used for chronic pain and OUD maintenance (daily observed dosing). Tapentadol: µ-agonist + NE reuptake inhibition → neuropathic + nociceptive pain.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Opioid prescribing principles: use lowest effective dose, regular (ATC) dosing for chronic pain (not PRN), combine with adjuvants (paracetamol, NSAIDs, gabapentinoids), reassess regularly. ALWAYS prescribe laxative concurrently (stimulant ± osmotic; constipation universal, no tolerance). Morphine starting dose: opioid-naive adult 5-10 mg IR PO q4h (or 30-60 mg/day); elderly/renal impairment 2-5 mg q4-6h. ER formulations (MST Continus 12-hrly): calculate total daily IR requirement → convert to ER with same dose PRN breakthrough (1/6th of daily dose). Breakthrough dose: 1/6th of total daily dose, as needed (max hourly if needed). Conversion between opioids (equianalgesic dosing): Oral morphine 30 mg ≈ oral oxycodone 15-20 mg ≈ oral hydromorphone 6 mg ≈ IV morphine 10 mg ≈ transdermal fentanyl 12-25 mcg/hr. Reduce conversion dose by 25-50% when switching (incomplete cross-tolerance). Renal impairment: morphine accumulates (M-6-G) → respiratory depression; prefer fentanyl, hydromorphone, buprenorphine. Opioid adverse effects: Respiratory depression (treat with naloxone 0.4-2 mg IV q2-3 min; naloxone t½ shorter than most opioids → re-narcotization possible → repeat doses or infusion); Pruritus (H1 release with morphine → antihistamine; or low-dose naloxone infusion); Nausea/vomiting (first 1-2 weeks — domperidone, ondansetron, haloperidol); Urinary retention (α-blocker or catheter); Delirium/cognitive impairment (opioid rotation, dose reduction, haloperidol for delirium). Opioid use disorder (OUD): buprenorphine/naloxone (Suboxone) sublingual preferred maintenance therapy. Naltrexone (once-daily or monthly injection) for sustained abstinence after detox. Naloxone take-home programs: provide to all patients on high-dose opioids and to caregivers.

Entamoeba histolytica: ingestion of infective cysts (feco-oral route, contaminated food/water) → trophozoites in colon → colonic invasion (penetrate mucosa → 'flask-shaped' ulcers → bloody/mucoid diarrhoea, colitis) → hepatic seeding via portal blood → amoebic liver abscess (single large right lobe abscess; fever + RUQ pain + no intestinal symptoms in 50%). P01B ANTIMALARIALS
ANTIMALARIALS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Malaria: Plasmodium species (falciparum — most lethal, SE Asia, Africa; vivax — worldwide; malariae; ovale; knowlesi — zoonotic) transmitted by female Anopheles mosquito. Life cycle: sporozoites injected → hepatic stage (exoerythrocytic replication — silent; P. vivax/ovale form hypnozoites — dormant forms causing relapse weeks-years later) → merozoites released → red blood cell invasion → asexual erythrocytic cycle (trophozoite → schizont → merozoite release: 48-72 hr cyclical fever) + sexual gametocytes (infect mosquito → sexual reproduction → new sporozoites). P. falciparum pathogenesis: infected RBC cytoadherence to microvascular endothelium (PfEMP1 protein-ICAM-1/CD36/chondroitin sulfate A receptor) → microvascular obstruction → organ sequestration → cerebral malaria (coma, seizures), acute lung injury, AKI, severe anaemia, hypoglycaemia. Parasite digests haemoglobin in food vacuole → haem (toxic) → polymerised to haemozoin (inert). Artemisinin-Resistant P. falciparum emerging in Greater Mekong Subregion → treatment failure risk.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Chloroquine: lysosomotropic → concentrates in food vacuole → inhibits haem polymerisation → toxic haem accumulates → parasite lysis. Resistance (P. falciparum chloroquine resistance transporter CRT mutation) widespread. Still effective for P. vivax in many regions. Artemisinin and derivatives (artesunate, artemether, dihydroartemisinin — ACTs): endoperoxide bridge activated by haem Fe²⁺ → alkylating free radical → oxidative damage to parasite proteins + haem interaction → rapid parasite clearance. Artesunate IV: drug of choice for severe P. falciparum. ACTs (artemether-lumefantrine [Coartem/Riamet], artesunate-amodiaquine, artesunate-mefloquine): artemisinin clears rapidly (short t½ 1-2 hrs) + partner drug (longer t½) eliminates residual parasites → combination prevents resistance. Quinine + doxycycline: inhibit haem polymerisation (quinine) + protein synthesis (doxycycline); IV quinine in severe malaria if artesunate unavailable. Primaquine: 8-aminoquinoline; kills P. vivax/ovale hepatic hypnozoites (only drug class that clears liver stage — radical cure); also kills gametocytes (↓ transmission). Pyrimethamine + sulphadoxine (SP/Fansidar): DHFR + DHPS inhibition (folate antagonism) → used for intermittent preventive treatment in pregnancy (IPTp) + children (IPTc) in Africa. Resistance widespread for treatment. Mefloquine: haem polymerisation inhibition; long t½ (2-4 weeks); chemoprophylaxis. CNS adverse effects (nightmares, anxiety, psychosis). Atovaquone-proguanil (Malarone): electron transport chain disruption (atovaquone) + proguanil synergy (DHFR inhibition); prophylaxis + treatment; well-tolerated; expensive.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Uncomplicated P. falciparum (first-line per Rwanda/WHO guidelines): Artemether-lumefantrine (Coartem) 6-dose regimen: 4 tabs at diagnosis, 4 tabs 8 hrs later, then 4 tabs BID x 2 days. Take with fatty meal (↑ lumefantrine absorption ↑ 16x with fat). Complete full course even if asymptomatic. Severe P. falciparum: IV artesunate 2.4 mg/kg at 0, 12, 24 hrs → then daily until oral tolerated → switch to oral ACT. Quinine IV (10 mg/kg q8h) if artesunate unavailable. Intensive monitoring: glucose q1-2 hrs (quinine → hyperinsulinaemia → hypoglycaemia), ECG (quinine → QTc prolongation), fluid balance (over-hydration causes pulmonary oedema in severe malaria — restrict to maintenance), haematocrit, consciousness. Exchange transfusion no longer recommended. P. vivax/ovale radical cure: chloroquine 25 mg/kg over 3 days + primaquine 15 mg OD x 14 days (radical cure). Primaquine: check G6PD status before prescribing (primaquine → oxidant haemolysis → severe haemolytic anaemia in G6PD-deficient patients — common in Africa/Asia/Mediterranean, up to 30% males). If G6PD deficient: 45 mg primaquine once weekly x 8 weeks (safer regimen) or withhold. Pregnancy: chloroquine safe; artemisinin-based ACTs used from 2nd trimester; primaquine contraindicated in pregnancy (G6PD uncertain in fetus). IPTp: SP 3 doses (at 16, 28, 36 weeks) for pregnant women in endemic areas. Malaria prophylaxis for travellers (outbound): atovaquone-proguanil (most tolerable, no G6PD concern), doxycycline, or mefloquine depending on destination, duration, tolerability. ALWAYS check updated travel medicine guidelines (CDC, WHO, TMVC) for resistance patterns by specific region. Chemoprophylaxis must start before travel, continue during, and after return (product-specific timing).

P02BA ANTITREMATODALS
ANTHELMINTICS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Helminth (worm) infections — highly prevalent in tropical/subtropical resource-limited settings. Nematodes (roundworms): Ascaris lumbricoides — intestinal obstruction, malnutrition, lung (Löffler syndrome); Hookworms (Ancylostoma, Necator) — iron-deficiency anaemia (blood-sucking); Trichuris trichiura (whipworm) — dysentery, rectal prolapse; Strongyloides stercoralis — auto-infection cycle, hyperinfection in immunocompromised (potentially fatal dissemination). Filariae (tissue nematodes): Wuchereria bancrofti, Brugia (lymphatic filariasis → lymphoedema, elephantiasis); Onchocerca volvulus (river blindness — microfilariae in cornea → inflammatory scarring → blindness). Cestodes (tapeworms): Taenia solium (pork tapeworm → cysticercosis — larval cysts in brain → seizures, raised ICP), Taenia saginata (beef tapeworm), Hymenolepis nana, Echinococcus (hydatid disease). Trematodes (flukes): Schistosoma (bilharzia) — granulomatous hepatic fibrosis, portal hypertension, haematuria (S. haematobium), bladder carcinoma.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Benzimidazoles (mebendazole, albendazole, thiabendazole): bind β-tubulin of nematode (parasite-selective) → inhibit tubulin polymerisation → microtubule disruption → ↓ glucose uptake → energy depletion → helminth death. Albendazole also kills tissue larval stages (cysticercosis, echinococcosis) — better GI absorption than mebendazole (enhanced with fatty meal). Ivermectin: binds glutamate-gated Cl⁻ channels (invertebrate-specific, absent in mammals with intact BBB) → ↑ Cl⁻ permeability → hyperpolarisation → paralysis → death of nematodes and ectoparasites. Drug of choice for Strongyloides, onchocerciasis, loiasis, filariasis. Praziquantel: ↑ Ca²⁺ permeability in schistosome/cestode membranes → paralysis + tegument damage → phagocytosis by host immune cells. Drug of choice for ALL trematodes (schistosomiasis, liver flukes) and most cestodes (taeniasis, Hymenolepis). Pyrantel pamoate: depolarising NMJ blockade in nematodes (nicotinic ACh receptor agonist → spastic paralysis).
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Soil-transmitted helminths (Ascaris, hookworm, Trichuris): albendazole 400 mg single dose (all ages ≥2 yrs; mebendazole 500 mg single dose alternative). Repeat at 2 weeks for heavier infections. Strongyloides: ivermectin 200 mcg/kg OD x 2 days (minimum); repeat at 2 weeks; in immunocompromised/hyperinfection: daily ivermectin until stool/serology clears (can be prolonged). Ivermectin: enhanced absorption with food. Caution in Loa loa co-endemic areas (loiasis — ivermectin can cause severe neurological adverse events in patients with high Loa loa microfilaraemia > 30,000/mL — assess/test before treatment). Neurocysticercosis: albendazole 15 mg/kg/day + dexamethasone (inflammatory reaction on cyst death → cerebral oedema → seizures/raised ICP; steroid + antiepileptic mandatory before/during treatment). Controversy — active cysts only benefit from drug; inactive/calcified — no treatment needed. Neuroimaging essential (MRI) before treatment. Schistosomiasis: praziquantel 40 mg/kg single dose (mansoni/japonicum) or 60 mg/kg in 2-3 doses (haematobium). Take with food. Repeat dose at 2-4 weeks for heavy burden. Mass drug administration (MDA) programmes: albendazole/ivermectin for filariasis elimination; praziquantel for schistosomiasis. Hydatid disease (Echinococcus): primarily surgical (PAIR — percutaneous aspiration/injection/re-aspiration) ± albendazole 400 mg BID x 28-day cycles (3-6 months); albendazole pre/post-PAIR to reduce recurrence.

P03 ECTOPARASITICIDES, INCLUDING SCABICIDES, INSECTICIDES AND REPELLENTS
ECTOPARASITICIDES
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Ectoparasites infest the skin surface. Scabies: Sarcoptes scabiei var. hominis mite burrows into stratum corneum → lays eggs → intensely pruritic sensitisation reaction (IgE + cell-mediated). Highly contagious (close skin-to-skin contact). Norwegian (crusted) scabies: hyperinfection in immunocompromised → thousands of mites → scales, minimal itch (blunted immune response). Pediculosis (lice): Pediculus humanus capitis (head lice), P. humanus corporis (body lice — vector for typhus, relapsing fever, trench fever), Phthirus pubis (pubic/crab lice). Feed on blood → local reaction, secondary infection. Environmental insecticide resistance common (permethrin/pyrethroid resistance in some populations).
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Permethrin 5% cream: synthetic pyrethroid → binds Na⁺ channels in arthropod nerve membranes → sustained depolarisation → paralysis → death. Scabies: applied from neck down (head in infants/elderly/immunocompromised) x 8-12 hrs → wash off. Malathion 0.5%: organophosphate → acetylcholinesterase inhibition in insects → ACh accumulation → cholinergic toxidrome → death. Aqueous (alcohol-free) formulations for children/eczema. Ivermectin (oral): as P02 — also kills scabies mites (licensed for Norwegian scabies and permethrin-resistant scabies). Benzyl benzoate 25% lotion: alternative for scabies; cheaper but more irritating.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Scabies: permethrin 5% cream from neck to toes (pay special attention to finger webs, wrists, genitalia, umbilicus); wash off after 8-12 hrs; repeat in 7 days (kills hatched larvae from eggs that survived 1st treatment — eggs permethrin-resistant). ALL household contacts and sexual partners treated simultaneously (to prevent reinfection — critical). Treat clothing/bedding (60°C wash or seal in bag 72 hrs). Post-scabetic itch may persist 2-6 weeks after successful treatment (delayed hypersensitivity reaction) — oral antihistamines + hydrocortisone 1% cream; do NOT re-treat unnecessarily (inappropriate repeat treatment does not help). Norwegian (crusted) scabies: systemic ivermectin 200 mcg/kg + topical permethrin; ISOLATE patient (highly contagious); treat all contacts regardless of symptoms. Head lice: permethrin 1% lotion 10-min application, repeat in 7-10 days; OR malathion 0.5% aqueous 8-12 hr application. 'Bug-busting': wet combing with fine-tooth comb every 3-4 days x 2 weeks (alternative — no insecticides). Nit (egg) comb essential regardless of treatment (dead nits remain until physically removed). Treat all household members with live lice simultaneously. Body lice: treat clothing/bedding (lice live in seams, not on skin); personal hygiene; insecticidal treatment of skin if needed.

R01AA SYMPATOMIMETICS, PLAIN
NASAL PREPARATIONS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Allergic rhinitis (AR): IgE-mediated type I hypersensitivity to aeroallergens (pollen, house dust mite, pet dander, mould). Phase 1 (early, within minutes): allergen crosslinks mast cell surface IgE → degranulation → histamine, tryptase, PGD2, LTC4 release → nasal congestion (vascular engorgement), rhinorrhoea (glandular secretion), sneezing (afferent nerve stimulation), pruritus. Phase 2 (late, 4-8 hrs): cytokine-driven eosinophil/T-cell influx → nasal hyperresponsiveness, persistent congestion. Rhinosinusitis: viral (98%: rhinovirus, coronavirus, RSV) or bacterial (1-2%: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, M. catarrhalis) mucosa inflammation of sinonasal passages → sinus ostia obstruction → secondary bacterial superinfection. Nasal polyps: benign inflammatory outgrowths from sinonasal mucosa (eosinophilic, type 2 inflammation) associated with AR, ASA-exacerbated respiratory disease (AERD), CRS.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Intranasal corticosteroids (INCS — fluticasone, mometasone, budesonide, beclomethasone, triamcinolone): topical glucocorticoid receptor activation → ↓ nasal mucosal inflammation (↓ eosinophils, mast cells, IL-4, IL-5, TNF-α) → ↓ all nasal symptoms (congestion, rhinorrhoea, sneezing, pruritus) + ↓ nasal polyp size. Minimal systemic bioavailability (fluticasone <1%) — best safety profile. Require 1-2 weeks to achieve maximum effect. Topical decongestants (oxymetazoline, xylometazoline): α1/α2-adrenergic agonists → nasal mucosal vasoconstriction (venous capacitance vessels contract → ↓ congestion). Rapid onset (5-10 min). Rebound congestion (rhinitis medicamentosa) after >3-5 days use (α receptor upregulation + ↓ endogenous NE). Systemic decongestants (pseudoephedrine, phenylephrine): systemic α-adrenergic effect on nasal mucosa; cardiovascular stimulation risk. Cromones (sodium cromoglicate): mast cell membrane stabilisation (inhibit Cl⁻ channels → prevent Ca²⁺ influx → ↓ degranulation); weakly effective; multiple daily dosing. Topical antihistamines (azelastine, levocabastine): H1 block in nasal mucosa; some anti-inflammatory activity; faster onset than INCS.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Intranasal steroids: 1st-line for persistent/moderate-severe AR and nasal polyps. Proper technique: blow nose before use; prime pump (5-7 actuations first use); tilt head forward slightly; aim spray AWAY from septum (toward lateral nasal wall) to prevent septal perforation (uncommon but reported); sniff gently. Consistent daily use required (not PRN); reassess at 4-6 weeks. Fluticasone furoate (Avamys), mometasone (Nasonex): once-daily; minimal systemic absorption. Side effects: local — dryness, epistaxis (use saline spray before INCS, avoid digital trauma), rare septal perforation. In children: monitor growth velocity (systemic absorption with high doses — fluticasone propionate safer in children due to lower systemic bioavailability). Topical decongestants: max 3-5 consecutive days use ONLY. Use ONLY for specific severe acute congestion (sinusitis, nasal procedure preparation). Rhinitis medicamentosa: abrupt cessation + intranasal steroid cover + oral prednisolone taper (short course) for severe withdrawal congestion. Systemic decongestants: avoid with MAOIs (hypertensive crisis), thyroid disease, hypertension, cardiac disease; limited efficacy evidence for sinusitis. Saline irrigations (isotonic/hypertonic): safe, inexpensive, effective mucociliary clearance in both CRS and AR — promote daily use. Immunotherapy (allergen desensitisation): subcutaneous (SCIT) or sublingual (SLIT) — modifies AR course, reduces polysensitisation and asthma development. Reserved for moderate-severe AR inadequately controlled by pharmacotherapy.

R03AA.  DRUGS FOR OBSTRUCTIVE AIRWAY DISEASES
DRUGS FOR OBSTRUCTIVE AIRWAY DISEASES (ASTHMA AND COPD)
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Asthma: chronic inflammatory airway disease. Atopic asthma (most common): repeated allergen exposure → TH2 response → IL-4 (IgE class switching), IL-5 (eosinophil activation), IL-13 (goblet cell metaplasia, mucus hypersecretion) + mast cell degranulation → airway inflammation, oedema, mucus plugging, smooth muscle hypertrophy/hyperresponsiveness. Bronchoconstriction reversible (spontaneously or with bronchodilators). Airway remodelling (chronic): subepithelial fibrosis, smooth muscle hypertrophy → partially fixed obstruction. Triggers: allergens, exercise, cold air, viral URTI, aspirin/NSAIDs (AERD: COX-1 inhibition → ↓ PGE2 → ↑ lipoxygenase → ↑ cysteinyl leukotrienes → bronchoconstriction), stress, pollution. COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease): primarily smoking-induced (also biomass fuel exposure); progressive airflow limitation, incompletely reversible. Two components: (1) Emphysema — protease-antiprotease imbalance (cigarette smoke → macrophage/neutrophil activation → elastase/MMP excess → alveolar wall destruction → airspace enlargement → ↓ elastic recoil → expiratory flow limitation); (2) Chronic bronchitis — goblet cell hyperplasia → mucus hypersecretion → cough, sputum. Airway hyperresponsiveness, inflammation (neutrophil-dominant, ↑ IL-8, TNF-α — different from asthma). COPD exacerbations: triggered by URTI, air pollution → inflammatory cascade → ↓ FEV1, dyspnoea, ↑ sputum, acute respiratory failure risk.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Short-acting β2-agonists (SABAs — salbutamol/albuterol, terbutaline, fenoterol): β2-adrenergic receptor (Gs-cAMP-PKA) activation in airway smooth muscle → ↓ intracellular Ca²⁺ (via MLCK inhibition + ↓ IP3-mediated release) → bronchial smooth muscle relaxation. Also ↑ mucociliary clearance, ↓ mediator release. Rapid onset (5 min), duration 4-6 hrs. Long-acting β2-agonists (LABAs — salmeterol, formoterol, vilanterol): same mechanism; duration 12-24 hrs (lipophilic anchoring to plasma membrane). Formoterol: full agonist, faster onset — can be used as reliever in MART (maintenance AND reliever therapy). Short-acting muscarinic antagonists (SAMAs — ipratropium): block M3 muscarinic receptors on airway smooth muscle + mucus glands → ↓ bronchoconstriction + ↓ secretion. 15-30 min onset. Complement β2-agonists (additive bronchodilation via different pathway). Long-acting muscarinic antagonists (LAMAs — tiotropium, umeclidinium, glycopyrronium, aclidinium): once-daily M3 blockade; tiotropium kinetically selective (rapid M2 — which normally brakes acetylcholine release — re-association + slow M3 re-association). Most effective bronchodilator class in COPD. Inhaled corticosteroids (ICS — budesonide, fluticasone propionate, beclomethasone dipropionate, ciclesonide, mometasone): glucocorticoid receptor activation in airway epithelium/smooth muscle → ↓ eosinophilic inflammation (↓ IL-4, IL-5, IL-13 cytokines, ↓ mast cells, ↓ airway oedema). Cornerstone of asthma management (not COPD — unless frequent exacerbations with blood eosinophilia). Leukotriene receptor antagonists (LTRAs — montelukast, zafirlukast): block CysLT1 receptors (expressed on bronchial smooth muscle, eosinophils, mast cells) → ↓ leukotriene C4/D4/E4-mediated bronchoconstriction, ↑ mucus, ↑ eosinophilia. Adjunct to ICS in asthma; particularly useful: AERD, exercise-induced asthma, allergic rhinitis comorbidity, paediatric asthma. Methylxanthines (theophylline, aminophylline): non-selective PDE inhibition → ↑ cAMP/cGMP → bronchodilation + anti-inflammatory. Also adenosine receptor antagonism. Narrow therapeutic index (TDM required). IV aminophylline: for severe COPD exacerbations/asthma (limited evidence; IV salbutamol + ipratropium preferred). Biologics (dupilumab, mepolizumab, benralizumab, tezepelumab): for severe eosinophilic/type-2 refractory asthma; target IL-4R/IL-13R (dupilumab), IL-5/IL-5R (mepolizumab/benralizumab), TSLP (tezepelumab).
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
INHALER TECHNIQUE IS CRITICAL: up to 70% of patients use inhalers incorrectly → subtherapeutic drug delivery. Check and re-check technique at every visit using placebo device. pMDI (pressurised metered-dose inhaler): shake before use; exhale to functional residual capacity; place mouthpiece in mouth (lips sealed) or just outside; actuate at start of slow deep inhalation (2-3 sec) over 3-5 seconds; breath-hold 10 sec; wait 30 sec between puffs. Spacers/VHC (valved holding chambers): MANDATORY for all pMDI users unable to coordinate (young children, elderly, severe dyspnoea); reduces oropharyngeal deposition → ↓ candidiasis/dysphonia with ICS; ↑ lung deposition 3-4x. DPI (dry powder inhaler): device-specific loading → exhale AWAY from device → seal lips → FAST forceful inhalation (high inspiratory flow required: ≥30-60 L/min — inadequate in severe exacerbation). SMI (soft mist inhaler — Respimat): slow mist → less coordinate effort needed. Asthma step-up management (GINA guidelines 2023): Step 1: SABA PRN (low-dose formoterol/budesonide as preferred reliever). Step 2: low-dose ICS + PRN SABA OR LTRA alone. Step 3: low-dose ICS/LABA. Step 4: medium-to-high ICS/LABA. Step 5: add LAMA, biologic assessment, consider high-dose ICS. NEVER prescribe LABA alone in asthma (INCREASE IN ASTHMA MORTALITY — must always use with ICS, usually fixed-dose combination inhaler). Preferred approach: single ICS/formoterol inhaler as both maintenance and reliever (MART/SMART strategy) — reduces exacerbations vs fixed-dose ICS + separate SABA. ICS after use: rinse mouth and gargle immediately (↓ oropharyngeal candidiasis and dysphonia — topical deposition issue). Systemic effects minimal with standard doses (fluticasone + budesonide — less systemic than beclomethasone/triamcinolone). Regular use essential (daily, not PRN). Oral steroids for exacerbations: prednisolone 40-50 mg/day x 5-7 days (no taper for short courses). COPD management (GOLD guidelines): All COPD: smoking cessation (most effective intervention — slows FEV1 decline), influenza + pneumococcal vaccines, pulmonary rehabilitation. GOLD A (low risk, less symptoms): SABA or SAMA PRN. GOLD B (low risk, more symptoms): LAMA or LABA (or LAMA + LABA combination). GOLD E (high risk of exacerbations): LAMA + LABA (if eos >300/μL → add ICS). Tiotropium: 1st-line LAMA in COPD; ↓ exacerbations, ↑ exercise tolerance. Roflumilast (PDE4 inhibitor, oral): reduces COPD exacerbations in severe emphysema with chronic bronchitis; significant GI side effects. Theophylline: narrow TW (5-15 mcg/mL); CYP1A2 metabolised — numerous drug interactions (check all); smoking ↑ metabolism; signs toxicity: nausea, tremor, arrhythmia, seizures. COPD exacerbation acute management: controlled oxygen (target SpO2 88-92% — avoid O2-driven hypercapnic respiratory failure), nebulised salbutamol + ipratropium, systemic prednisolone, antibiotics if purulent sputum/CRP elevated (amoxicillin or doxycycline), NIV (BiPAP) if hypercapnia (pH <7.35, pCO2 >6 kPa). COPD at end-stage: oxygen therapy, palliative care referral, opioids for dyspnoea, advance care planning.

R05CA EXPECTORENTS
COUGH AND EXPECTORANT PREPARATIONS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Cough is a protective reflex mediated by afferent signals from: tracheal/bronchial rapidly adapting stretch receptors (RARs — irritant receptors), slowly adapting stretch receptors (SARs), and C-fibre nociceptors (subepithelial → activated by chemical irritants, bradykinin, capsaicin). Signals via vagus (SLN + recurrent laryngeal) → NTS in medulla → cough reflex motor output (inspiration → glottic closure → rapid expiration). Productive cough (wet/chesty): excess mucus requires clearance (beneficial — suppress with caution). Non-productive (dry/irritative): no benefit, may cause sleep disturbance, rib fracture, syncope. Mucus hypersecretion: goblet cell hyperplasia (chronic bronchitis, asthma, PCD) → ↑ viscoelastic gel layer of mucus → impaired mucociliary clearance → mucus stasis → infection risk.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Expectorants (guaifenesin, ipecacuanha): ↑ respiratory tract fluid secretion (guaifenesin → ↑ submucosal gland and goblet cell secretion via gastric vagal reflex) → ↓ mucus viscosity → easier expectoration. Evidence modest (subjective symptom improvement). Mucolytics: N-acetylcysteine (NAC): reduces disulfide bonds in mucus glycoproteins → ↓ mucus viscosity + ↑ glutathione (antioxidant) → anti-inflammatory; also used in paracetamol overdose. Carbocisteine, erdosteine: modify mucus oligosaccharide structure → ↓ viscoelasticity. Bromhexine: ↑ mucus fluidity + ↑ mucociliary transport; active metabolite ambroxol (more potent); ambroxol also stimulates surfactant production. Dornase alfa (DNase): cleaves extracellular DNA (from dead neutrophils — main component of purulent mucus) → ↓ sputum viscosity (CF, bronchiectasis). Cough suppressants (antitussives): Dextromethorphan (DXM): NMDA receptor antagonist + σ1 receptor agonist → elevates cough threshold centrally; minimal sedation at therapeutic doses. CYP2D6 metabolized. Codeine phosphate: µ-opioid receptor agonist in NTS/nucleus ambiguus → ↓ cough reflex sensitivity; sedating; constipation; abuse potential. Benzonatate: structurally related to procaine; anaesthetises stretch receptors in lungs/pleura → reduces cough frequency (peripheral mechanism). Pholcodine: opioid antitussive (less addictive than codeine; less constipating).
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Investigate and treat underlying cause of cough first: ACE inhibitor cough (switch to ARB), asthma (inhaler), GERD (PPI), post-nasal drip (INCS), whooping cough (azithromycin), TB (refer). Do NOT suppress productive cough in COPD/bronchiectasis (impairs clearance). Guaifenesin: 200-400 mg q4-6h with adequate fluids (8+ glasses/day — critical for expectorant efficacy); limited evidence but well-tolerated. NAC oral (600 mg/day): evidence in COPD for reducing exacerbations (BRONCUS, PANTHEON trials); mucolytic in CF, bronchiectasis. Ambroxol: available oral/inhalation/IV; nebulised ambroxol in NICU for surfactant-deficient infants. Dextromethorphan: 15-30 mg q4-6h (max 120 mg/day); OTC in most countries; do NOT use in <12 years (caution in 12-18); serotonin syndrome risk with MAOIs (combination absolutely contraindicated — severe risk). High doses: dissociative effects (abuse risk — DXM 'robotripping'). Codeine cough preparations: avoid in children <12 years (post-tonsillectomy/adenoidectomy fatalities); restrict to short-term use; caution with other CNS depressants. Counsel: most acute cough (URI) is self-limiting (3-4 weeks viral URI cough resolves spontaneously). Antibiotics do NOT reduce duration of cough in acute bronchitis (viral). Red flags requiring urgent investigation: haemoptysis, weight loss, night sweats (TB/malignancy), hoarse voice, dysphagia, cough persisting >8 weeks.

R06A ANTIHISTAMINES FOR SYSTEMIC USE
ANTIHISTAMINES FOR SYSTEMIC USE
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Allergic rhinitis, urticaria, angioedema, atopic eczema pruritus: as N05B/D04/R01 above. Mast cell and basophil IgE crosslinking → degranulation → histamine release from cytoplasmic granules. Histamine acts on: H1 receptors (smooth muscle contraction, ↑ vascular permeability, pruritus, pain, bronchoconstriction, CNS arousal via histaminergic tuberomammillary neurons), H2 receptors (gastric acid secretion, cardiac effects), H3 receptors (presynaptic autoreceptors — ↓ histamine synthesis/release), H4 receptors (immune cell chemotaxis, pruritus). Urticaria: IgE-mediated mast cell activation in dermis → localised oedema (wheal and flare). Angioedema: deeper submucosal/subcutaneous oedema (potential airway compromise if laryngeal). Anaphylaxis: systemic mast cell/basophil activation → cardiovascular collapse (↓ SVR, ↑ vascular permeability → distributive shock), bronchospasm, angioedema.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
H1 antihistamines: competitive, reversible antagonists at H1 receptors. First-generation (sedating) H1-antihistamines (chlorpheniramine, diphenhydramine, promethazine, hydroxyzine, cyproheptadine): lipophilic → cross BBB → sedation (H1 antagonism in histaminergic arousal pathways) + anticholinergic (M1 blockade → dry mouth, urinary retention, constipation, blurred vision, confusion) effects. Significant CNS depression. Promethazine: also D2 and 5-HT2 antagonism → antiemetic. Second-generation (non-sedating) H1-antihistamines (cetirizine, loratadine, fexofenadine, desloratadine, levocetirizine, bilastine, rupatadine): lipophobic → P-gp efflux at BBB → minimal CNS penetration → minimal sedation. Longer duration (once-daily). Cetirizine: some anti-eosinophil activity (mild immunomodulatory effect); minor sedation in some individuals. Fexofenadine: P-glycoprotein substrate → virtually no CNS effects (safest for driving). Rupatadine: also PAF (platelet-activating factor) antagonism.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Second-generation antihistamines: preferred for chronic conditions (AR, chronic urticaria) due to better safety/tolerability profile. Once daily (loratadine, fexofenadine, desloratadine, bilastine) for better adherence. Fexofenadine: avoid grapefruit juice and fruit juices (↓ OATP-mediated absorption). Cetirizine: may cause drowsiness in some patients despite being 2nd generation — caution with driving until individual response known. Loratadine: virtually no sedation; extensively CYP-metabolised. Bilastine: no food within 1 hr of dose (impairs absorption). First-generation: sedation used therapeutically (short-term insomnia, procedural sedation). AVOID in: elderly (Beers criteria: falls, delirium, urinary retention, confusion), glaucoma (anticholinergic → acute closure crisis), BPH (urinary retention), myasthenia gravis. Do NOT drive/operate machinery. Hydroxyzine: sedating, anxiolytic and antipruritic; useful in acute urticaria/eczema pruritus (25-50 mg TID-QID). Antihistamines for acute allergic reactions/urticaria: SECOND-LINE to epinephrine in anaphylaxis (antihistamines do NOT prevent/reverse cardiovascular collapse or bronchospasm — epinephrine 0.5 mg IM thigh is life-saving first-line treatment). Cetirizine/fexofenadine for non-life-threatening reactions. Chronic spontaneous urticaria: regular daily 2nd-gen antihistamine (not PRN) up to 4x standard dose before considering additional therapy (ciclosporin, omalizumab). Avoid alcohol with all antihistamines (additive CNS depression).

S01AA EYE ANTIBIOTICS
OPHTHALMIC ANTIINFECTIVES AND ANTI-INFLAMMATORY
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Conjunctivitis: hyperaemia, discharge, itching; bacterial (Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, H. influenzae, N. gonorrhoeae neonatal), viral (adenoviral epidemic keratoconjunctivitis — highly contagious; HSV, molluscum), allergic (mast cell degranulation in conjunctiva). Keratitis: corneal infection → painful, photophobic, vision-threatening (HSV — dendritic ulcer with fluorescein; bacterial — contact lens wearers → Pseudomonas acuginosa keratitis; Acanthamoeba — painful, contact lens). Uveitis (anterior, posterior, panuveitis): inflammation of uveal tract — 50% associated with systemic disease (HLA-B27 spondyloarthropathy, sarcoidosis, Behçet's, JIA). Cataracts, post-surgical inflammation, macular oedema. Glaucoma: primary open-angle glaucoma (POAG) — optic nerve damage from ↑ IOP (normal: 10-21 mmHg); IOP elevated due to ↓ aqueous outflow (via trabecular meshwork → Schlemm's canal pathway compromised). Chronic, asymptomatic until significant visual field loss. Acute angle-closure glaucoma (AACG): rapid IOP spike (>30 mmHg) from pupillary block → painful red eye, blurred vision, halos, nausea — ophthalmic emergency.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Topical antibiotics (chloramphenicol, fusidic acid, neomycin/polymyxin, ciprofloxacin, tobramycin, azithromycin, ofloxacin): direct antimicrobial effect on conjunctival pathogens. Chloramphenicol: broad-spectrum; rare but documented aplastic anaemia with long-term systemic use (topical risk very low). Fluoroquinolones (ciprofloxacin, ofloxacin, levofloxacin): broad-spectrum including Pseudomonas; preferred for contact-lens wearers. Antivirals (acyclovir ointment, ganciclovir gel, trifluridine): thymidine kinase-dependent activation (as J05); HSV corneal treatment. Antifungal (natamycin suspension): polyene; for fungal keratitis. Topical NSAIDs (ketorolac, diclofenac, nepafenac): COX inhibition → ↓ PGE2 → ↓ post-operative/allergic inflammation; ↓ CME (cystoid macular oedema). Topical corticosteroids (dexamethasone, prednisolone acetate, fluorometholone, loteprednol etabonate): glucocorticoid receptor activation → ↓ inflammatory infiltrate (white cells, ↓ cytokines). Potency: dexamethasone > prednisolone > fluorometholone > loteprednol. Loteprednol: metabolically inactive metabolite → less IOP elevation, less cataract risk. Antiglaucoma preparations: Prostaglandin analogues (latanoprost, travoprost, bimatoprost, tafluprost): FP receptor agonism → matrix metalloproteinase activation → ↑ uveoscleral (non-trabecular) aqueous outflow. Most efficacious agent class; once-daily nocturnal dosing. Side effects: iris pigmentation changes (irreversible), eyelash growth (hypertrichosis), periorbital fat loss, conjunctival hyperaemia. Beta-blockers (timolol, betaxolol, levobunolol, carteolol): β1/β2-adrenergic blockade on ciliary epithelium → ↓ aqueous humour production. Timolol: non-selective (β1+β2) → systemic absorption possible → bradycardia, bronchospasm (CI in asthma/severe COPD). Betaxolol: β1-selective → safer in asthma. Carbonic anhydrase inhibitors (topical: dorzolamide, brinzolamide; systemic: acetazolamide): inhibit CA-II in ciliary epithelium → ↓ HCO₃⁻ + Na⁺ transport → ↓ aqueous production. Dorzolamide: stinging/bitter taste. Brinzolamide: suspension (less stinging). Oral acetazolamide for AACG emergency. Alpha-2 agonists (brimonidine, apraclonidine): ↓ aqueous production (adenylyl cyclase inhibition) + ↑ uveoscleral outflow. Also neuroprotective (experimental). Systemic absorption → ↓ BP, dry mouth, sedation. Apraclonidine: post-laser use only (tachyphylaxis limits long-term use). Rho kinase inhibitors (netarsudil): inhibit ROCK1/2 → ↑ trabecular meshwork outflow (↑ cell motility, ↓ cytoskeletal stiffness) + ↓ aqueous production. Miotics (pilocarpine): M3 agonist → ciliary muscle contraction → opens trabecular meshwork → ↑ outflow. Also pupil constriction (↓ AACG risk from pupillary block). Anticholinergics (atropine, cyclopentolate, tropicamide, homatropine): M3 antagonism → mydriasis (pupil dilation) + cycloplegia (ciliary muscle paralysis → loss of accommodation). Used for: cycloplegic refraction (accurate objective refraction in children), uveitis (prevent posterior synechiae from inflamed iris adhering to lens), fundus examination.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Eye drop administration: wash hands → tilt head back → gently pull lower lid down → instil 1 drop in lower fornix (not on cornea — blinks it out; lower fornix holds max 30 μL = 1 drop) → close eye gently (blinking pumps drops away) → nasolacrimal occlusion (press inner corner of eye for 1-2 min) → reduces systemic absorption 50-70% (critical for beta-blockers, brimonidine, timolol in children). Multiple drops: wait 5-10 min between each (first drop dilutes/washes out second drop if insufficient time). Soft contact lenses: remove before instilling eye drops; wait 15-30 min before reinserting (benzalkonium chloride [BAK] preservative absorbed into lens → corneal toxicity). Preservative-free formulations for patients using drops QID+, those with dry eye or contact lenses. Topical antibiotic for bacterial conjunctivitis: chloramphenicol 0.5% drops q2h initially (reducing to QID) + 1% ointment at night x 5-7 days. Or fusidic acid gel BID x 7 days. Viral conjunctivitis: no antibiotic (self-limiting 7-14 days); artificial tears; cold compresses; HIGHLY CONTAGIOUS — strict hand hygiene, no sharing towels. HSV keratitis: acyclovir 3% ointment 5 times daily x 10 days; NEVER use topical steroids in suspected active HSV (geographic ulceration → perforation). Refer ophthalmology. Topical steroids: NEVER initiate without confirming diagnosis (exclude active infection, corneal ulcer, HSV). May cause or worsen: steroid-induced glaucoma (↑ IOP in 30% genetically susceptible patients — monitor IOP after 2-4 weeks of steroid use), posterior subcapsular cataract (prolonged use), ↑ susceptibility to fungal/amoeba keratitis. Taper gradually. Fluorometholone and loteprednol: lower IOP elevation risk → preferred for long-term use. Glaucoma treatment: aim for 25-30% IOP reduction from baseline. Prostaglandin analogue (1st-line if no contraindication): instil at bedtime (maximum effect coincides with overnight ↑ IOP); store correctly (refrigerate some — check product). All glaucoma medications are life-long; adherence education critical (asymptomatic disease — patients often stop drops when no symptoms). Review IOP at 4-6 weeks. Add agents from different classes for insufficient IOP control. AACG emergency: IV acetazolamide 500 mg + oral glycerol/mannitol + pilocarpine 2% qid + timolol 0.5% + IV analgesia/antiemetics → urgent ophthalmology referral for peripheral iridotomy (laser or surgical). S02AA ANTI-INFECTIVES
OTOLOGICAL PREPARATIONS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Otitis externa (swimmer's ear): disrupted cerumen (earwax) protective barrier + humid environment → bacterial (Pseudomonas aeruginosa most common, S. aureus) or fungal (Aspergillus niger, Candida) colonization of external ear canal → oedema, inflammation, pain on tragus pressure, discharge. Risk factors: swimming, excessive ear cleaning/instrumentation, hearing aids, narrow canals, eczema. Otitis media with effusion (glue ear): Eustachian tube dysfunction → negative middle ear pressure → transudate accumulation → hearing loss (conductive) without acute infection. Acute otitis media (AOM): viral URTI → Eustachian tube inflammation/obstruction → bacterial superinfection (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, M. catarrhalis) in middle ear cleft → otalgia, fever, bulging TM (tympanic membrane) → possible TM perforation → purulent discharge.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Topical antibiotics (ofloxacin, ciprofloxacin, framycetin, neomycin, gentamicin): direct antimicrobial in ear canal/middle ear. Fluoroquinolones (ofloxacin, ciprofloxacin): most effective for OE; safe with perforated TM (non-ototoxic — unlike aminoglycosides). Aminoglycosides (neomycin, gentamicin): ototoxic if TM perforated or in middle ear — should NOT be used when TM integrity uncertain. Antifungal drops (clotrimazole 1%): for otomycosis. Topical steroids (fluocinolone, betamethasone, dexamethasone): reduce canal oedema and inflammation in otitis externa (particularly important when oedema prevents adequate antibiotic penetration — consider wick/aural toilet). Combination preparations (antibiotic + steroid): widely used; efficacy superior to single agents for OE with inflammation. Cerumenolytics (docusate sodium, olive oil, sodium bicarbonate, hydrogen peroxide): soften and dislodge impacted cerumen → easier removal.
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Ear drop instillation: patient warm drops to body temperature (cold drops → vertigo/nausea — caloric effect on vestibular system). Lie on side with affected ear upward. Instil prescribed number of drops → apply gentle pressure to tragus (pump drops inward) → remain lying for 5-10 min for adequate contact. Cotton wool plug after. Otitis externa: topical fluoroquinolone (ofloxacin/ciprofloxacin) drops QID x 7 days (superior and safer than aminoglycoside ± steroid). If severe canal oedema (drops cannot penetrate): insert expandable wick (pope wick/bismuth gauze) and drop onto wick. Keep ear DRY throughout treatment (no swimming; hair wash: cotton wool in ear). AOM treatment: pain relief first (paracetamol ± ibuprofen). Antibiotics in AOM: watchful waiting appropriate in mild cases ≥2 years age (many resolve without antibiotics in 2-3 days). Antibiotic if: bilateral AOM <2 years, severe symptoms (severe otalgia, high fever, systemically unwell), TM perforation, <6 months age, no improvement in 48-72 hrs. Amoxicillin 80-90 mg/kg/day (high dose for penicillin-resistant S. pneumoniae) x 5-7 days. Amoxicillin-clavulanate if no response at 48-72 hrs (covers β-lactamase producers). NEVER instil aminoglycoside drops (neomycin, gentamicin) into ears with perforated TM or grommets (tympanostomy tubes) — cochleotoxicity risk. Fluoroquinolone drops safe with perforated TM/grommets. Cerumen removal: soften with olive oil or sodium bicarbonate drops BID x 1 week → irrigation with warm water (body temperature); do NOT irrigate if history of perforation, previous ear surgery, or cholesteatoma.

S03AA. OPHTHALMOLOGICAL AND OTOLOGICAL PREPARATIONS
OPHTHALMOLOGICAL AND OTOLOGICAL COMBINED PREPARATIONS
■ PATHOPHYSIOLOGY & DISEASE MECHANISM
Combined antibiotic + steroid preparations balance dual need for antimicrobial coverage and anti-inflammatory effects in infected/inflamed conditions of eye and ear where both bacterial load reduction and inflammation control are simultaneously required.
■ PHARMACOLOGICAL PRINCIPLES
Combinations: corticosteroid (dexamethasone, hydrocortisone, prednisolone, betamethasone) + antibiotic (neomycin, polymyxin B, bacitracin, ofloxacin, tobramycin, ciprofloxacin, gramicidin, framycetin) ± antifungal. Mechanism = sum of individual components (see S01/S02).
■ CLINICAL DISPENSING GUIDANCE
Ophthalmic use: do NOT use any combined antibiotic-steroid preparation in undiagnosed 'red eye' without confirmed bacterial infection and exclusion of viral keratitis/corneal ulceration. Steroid can worsen active HSV, fungal, or amoeba infection catastrophically. Confirm diagnosis with slit-lamp examination (ideally ophthalmologist) before prescribing. Otic: beware aminoglycoside-containing preparations with perforated TM (ototoxic). Prefer fluoroquinolone + steroid combination if TM integrity uncertain. Limit combination preparations to specific indications; do not use as first-line for non-infected inflammatory conditions (steroid alone preferable; reduces aminoglycoside resistance pressure).
